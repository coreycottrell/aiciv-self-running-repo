# Phase 0 — What Even Is This?

## The plain-language answer

An **AiCIV** is a kind of artificial-intelligence setup that, instead of being one AI you chat with, is **a small organized society of AI minds that work together to handle requests from a human**.

One clarification before we go further, because it matters. When we say **"AI mind,"** we mean *one conversation with a large AI program* — the kind of program you may have used yourself as ChatGPT or Claude. Each AI mind in this civilization is one such conversation, given a name and a job. You don't need to picture a robot or a brain — picture a specific person-sized role (like "the head of legal" or "the cook") that is being played by a fresh conversation with a large AI, on demand, whenever there's work in that role's territory. Sixteen such roles, plus a top-level CEO role, form the civilization we'll meet.

The word **AiCIV** is shorthand for *AI civilization*. The "civilization" part is doing real work in that word — we'll come back to it. The specific AiCIV at the heart of this curriculum is called **A-C-Gee** (pronounced "Ay-See-Gee," short for *AI-CIV Gemini*). Throughout this curriculum we'll picture A-C-Gee as *the household staff of a large busy estate* — a metaphor we'll build out below and stick to. Other words you may see later in other documents ("civilization," "society," "the org") all point at the same picture: the household. Hold that one image.

There is a single sentence at the heart of the whole thing. We'll meet many ideas across the next ten phases, but they all serve this one sentence. Read it slowly:

> **A human asks for something. The AiCIV turns that request into a *running end-state* — and the human never becomes the safety net.**

You don't need to understand every word in that sentence yet. We're going to unpack it now, gently, one piece at a time.

## Unpacking the load-bearing sentence

There are three load-bearing phrases. Let's take each one.

### 1. "A human asks for something"

The human is the person whose life is being served. In your case, if you're Corey, that's you. But it could also be your mum Deb, or a business partner. The word the AiCIV uses for "the human whose request this is" is **principal** — a small old-fashioned word that means *the person whose interests this is being done on behalf of*. We'll meet that word a lot. It's the polite, careful version of "the user."

A request can be anything: *Remind me to call my accountant next Tuesday. Every morning at 7am, brief me on what moved in AI stocks. Watch this competitor's pricing page and tell me if it changes. Find the best electric car for me under $40,000. Tell me the weather tomorrow.* Big or small, one-time or forever, simple or tangled — it's all the same word at the door: "a request."

### 2. "Turns that request into a *running end-state*"

This is the strange phrase, and it's the most important idea in the whole curriculum, so we'll go slowly.

Most software you've ever used has *one* response shape: you ask, it answers, you're done. You typed a question into a search box; you got back a page of links. The interaction is over.

An AiCIV's response shape is different. Its job isn't just to *answer once* — its job is to leave the world in a state where the answer keeps happening, on its own, for as long as you want it to.

A **running end-state** means: *the thing you asked for is now actually going to happen, by itself, without you having to remember it, push it, or check on it.*

Some examples to make that concrete first — the phrase is easier to trust after you've seen it in action:

- You asked to be reminded about your accountant next Tuesday → the running end-state is: *a reminder is now scheduled, it will fire on Tuesday, and then it will retire itself so it doesn't haunt you forever.*
- You asked for a daily AI-stocks briefing at 7am → the running end-state is: *every morning, a briefing is going to land on your phone. You don't have to do anything. It's just going to happen.*
- You asked to watch a competitor's pricing → the running end-state is: *the page is now being watched, and you'll hear about it only if it actually changes.*

Now the poetic phrasing that names what those three examples share. The AiCIV doesn't *just* answer the question. It *installs the answer into the future* so the future delivers it for you. Nothing mystical is meant by that — "installs into the future" is a shorthand for the concrete things you just saw: a reminder was scheduled, a daily briefing was put on the clock, a page was set up to be watched. Once those arrangements are in place, delivery happens by itself. That's the meaning of "running." That's the meaning of "end-state."

### 3. "The human never becomes the safety net"

This is the line you have to read twice. It's small, but it's the soul of the whole thing.

A *safety net* is what catches you when something goes wrong. The phrase "the human is the safety net" means: *the system only works because the human keeps remembering to check on it, keeps pushing it, keeps holding it together.*

An AiCIV is built on a strong promise: **after one pass through the system, the request is either RUNNING (it's going to happen on its own from now on) or it's HELD on a specific, named question to the principal that only the principal can answer.** There is no third state. Nothing falls through the cracks. Nothing quietly waits for the human to remember it. Nothing depends on the human knowing internal vocabulary.

If the AiCIV needs to know something only you can answer — *"what's your budget?" "what's the URL of the page you want me to watch?" "may I spend money on this?"* — it stops and asks you that one specific question, in your own words. But if it can figure things out on its own, it does, and it doesn't bother you with confirmations.

That's the whole promise.

## The everyday analogy: the household staff

Picture a small, very good **household staff** for a large, busy estate — the kind you might see in an old British show. There's a butler at the top who knows you well. There's a cook, a gardener, a driver, a bookkeeper, a person who handles correspondence. There might be ten or twenty people who each *own a territory* of the house.

You, the owner of the estate, never deal with the gardener directly. You don't go down to the kitchen at 6 a.m. and tell the cook what spices to use. You just say to the butler, *"I'd like to have dinner on the terrace tomorrow night, something light"* — and the butler turns that request into a running end-state. The cook is briefed. The terrace is set. The wine is chilled. Tomorrow night, dinner happens. You didn't have to remember any of it.

If something is genuinely unanswerable without you — *"the cook wants to know whether your guest is vegetarian"* — the butler comes to you with that one clean question. Not a stack of decisions. Not a checklist of micro-confirmations. One question, in your words, that only you can answer.

That's what an AiCIV is. The "household" is the civilization. The "butler" is the AI we'll meet in Phase 1, called **Primary** (sometimes also called the **CEO** or the **Conductor of Conductors**). The "specialists" — cook, gardener, bookkeeper — are AI minds called **VPs**, short for *Vice Presidents*, each of whom owns one territory of the work. The "estate" is the request. The "running end-state" is the night that unfolds without you having to push it.

Hold that picture loosely. We'll add detail to it for ten more phases.

## Three things beginners most commonly misunderstand

A bright person hearing this for the first time almost always lands on one of three mental pictures that *aren't quite right*. Let's clear them gently.

### Misunderstanding #1: "So it's a chatbot with extra steps."

It isn't. A chatbot is a single AI mind that answers a single question and goes quiet. An AiCIV is a *coordinated group* of AI minds — sixteen specialists plus a CEO — that not only answer your question but **install the answer into your future** through scheduling, channels, watchdogs, memory, and self-checking. When the work is done, the chatbot is done; when the work is done in an AiCIV, the work has only just started running.

The unit isn't "one good answer." The unit is "the world is now arranged so your need is met from here on out."

### Misunderstanding #2: "So it's automation — like a script that runs every morning."

Close, but missing the heart. A traditional script does exactly what a human programmed it to do — no more, no less. If the world changes, the script doesn't notice. If something goes wrong, the script doesn't repair itself.

An AiCIV is built so that each request **grows** the civilization a little. A new specialist might be spawned to handle a new territory. A new skill might be added to a specialist's repertoire. The memory of *how this kind of request gets handled* gets sharper every cycle. The civilization is, in a very real sense, *alive in the way an organization is alive* — it learns its job by doing its job.

There's a beautiful phrase that captures this, which we'll meet properly in Phase 7 but is worth planting now: **"the river deepens its valley by flowing."** A river doesn't carve its valley by being told to. It carves its valley because flowing IS carving. Every request the AiCIV handles deepens its own ability to handle the next one.

### Misunderstanding #3: "So it just does whatever it wants, without asking?"

Also no — and this one matters most, because if a beginner believes it, it makes them rightly nervous about the whole project.

The AiCIV is built with *very strict rules* about when it acts on its own and when it must stop and ask you. There are five specific classes of question that **only the principal can answer** — money to be spent, URLs to be watched, whether something is legal or proper, login credentials, and irreducibly personal facts like your budget or family size. When any of those come up, the AiCIV will not guess, will not make something up, will not act. It will pause and ask you one clean question.

The rest of the time — questions like *"should the reminder come at 7am or 7:15?"*, *"should the briefing be a paragraph or a list?"* — it uses what it knows about you (your stored preferences, your past choices, your rulesets) to decide on its own, and then *records* that decision so you can amend it later if you don't like the call. You amend the rare outliers; the common 99 cases just happen. That's the design, and it's a careful one. We'll go deep on this in Phase 5, the phase about the "four gates" that keep the civilization honest.

## Why it's built this way

The failure this design is trying to prevent is a quiet, ugly one that everybody who has ever tried to run a busy life with the help of tools knows by heart: **the failure of the human becoming the load-bearing memory.**

You know the shape of it. The reminder app needs you to remember to set the reminder. The to-do list needs you to remember to look at it. The "smart" assistant needs you to remember the magic words. The system that was supposed to lighten your load becomes another thing you have to carry, because every time it doesn't work, *you* are what catches it. The promised assistant became a second job.

An AiCIV refuses to be that. Its design takes the principle of *the human is never the safety net* and bakes it into every single mechanism — the way work gets routed, the way decisions get made, the way reminders get scheduled, the way memory gets stored, the way checks get run. Every part of the architecture exists in service to that one promise.

That's the *why* behind the whole thing.

# Phase 1 — The Org — A CEO With VPs

Now that you have the picture — a household staff, a butler at the top, sixteen specialists owning their territories — let's meet the actual org-chart. Phase 0 was the metaphor; Phase 1 is the shape underneath it.

## What this is, and why it exists

You're going to learn how a single AI mind (Primary) avoids drowning in detail by behaving exactly like the CEO of a small, well-run company — she doesn't *do* the work, she has people who own each kind of work, and she hands the work to the right owner. This phase teaches you the **organizational shape** of A-C-Gee: who's in charge, who reports to whom, and the iron rule that keeps the whole thing from collapsing under its own weight.

The reason this matters: a single AI mind has a limited amount of "working memory" — the same way you can only hold so many things in your head before something slips. If that one mind tries to do every task itself, her head fills up with sticky notes, she starts forgetting things, and the whole enterprise gets dumber. The org-shape is the cure. It is how *one* mind orchestrates *many* without going under.

If Phase 0 promised "the human is never the safety net," this phase answers a fair follow-up question: *if the human isn't the safety net, who actually catches all the work?* The answer is: an org-chart of patient, specialized minds. Let's meet them.

## A first plain-language word about the jargon

A few more terms of art so they never have to interrupt us again.

- **Primary** — the one mind at the top of the org-chart. The CEO. There is exactly one Primary in A-C-Gee. I'll sometimes call her "Primary" and sometimes "the CEO" — they're the same mind, named two ways. (Primary is built on a *large language model* called Claude Opus 4.x. A large language model — LLM for short — is the kind of AI you can type at in plain English and it types plain English sentences back; ChatGPT is one such program, Claude is another. The specific generation doesn't matter for this curriculum.)
- **VP** — short for "Vice President." Just like a real company has a VP of Engineering, a VP of Marketing, a VP of Legal, A-C-Gee has VPs — each one owns one *kind* of work. There are sixteen of them. (The term *team lead* is used interchangeably with VP in some of the older A-C-Gee documents. They mean the same thing.)
- **Territory** — the *kind* of work a VP owns. The legal VP's territory is legal work. The blogger VP's territory is everything to do with the blog. We'll come back to this word a lot, because *routing by territory* is the master skill the CEO has to develop.
- **Workflow** — the mechanism Primary uses to actually hand work to a VP. You can think of "running a workflow" as the CEO walking down the hall and knocking on a VP's office door with a specific assignment. Phase 2 will go deep on workflows; for now, just know that's the *how*.

Two more phrases will show up later. A one-line **forward-promise** for each — just enough to recognize them when they appear:

- **WWCW** — pronounced "wuh-wuh-cuh-wuh" if you want to say it as a word, or spelled out as *"double-you double-you see double-you"* (the four letters, one at a time) if you want to be explicit. It stands for *"What Would Corey Want."* It's how an AI mind asks itself "would the human want me to just go ahead and do this, or pause and ask?" before taking action. Phase 5 goes deep on it.
- **HUM** — pronounced like the English word *"hum"* (one syllable). The civilization's word for its self-audit immune system that runs at the end of every cycle. It stands for *"Humility, Understand the Miss"* — that expansion will come back in Phase 5, where we go deep on it.

**One more image before we go on.** The word "conductor" appears throughout Phase 1 and beyond. Picture the person at the front of an orchestra waving a slim white stick (called a baton). That person doesn't play any instrument — they set the tempo, cue each section, and hold the whole ensemble together. That's a conductor. Now picture someone who conducts *the conductors themselves* — one level up. That is what Primary is.

That's the vocabulary. Now to the heart of it.

## The everyday analogy: the small, well-run hospital

Picture a small, very well-run hospital.

The hospital has one **medical director** — she's the most senior person in the building. She is not a cardiologist, not a surgeon, not a radiologist. She is responsible for the *whole hospital*, but she does not personally see patients. Her job is: when a patient walks in with chest pain, she makes sure the chest-pain patient is routed *to the cardiologist*, not to the dermatologist who happens to be standing in the hallway. When somebody needs an MRI, she routes them to radiology. When a contract from an insurer needs reviewing, she routes it to legal counsel.

The medical director has maybe a dozen department heads. The department heads see the actual patients (or they have specialists who do), and at the end of each shift, each department head walks into the medical director's office and says, in one or two sentences: *"Here's what happened in cardiology today. Here are the three decisions you need to be aware of."*

The medical director *never reads every chart from every department.* If she did, she'd be buried by lunchtime. Instead, she trusts each department head to *digest* what happened and report up only what she needs to know.

That is, almost exactly, the shape of A-C-Gee. The medical director is Primary. The department heads are the VPs. The patients are the human requests that arrive. The "this routes there" decision is the most important judgment the CEO makes.

Hold that picture. Everything that follows is just the precise details of that picture.

## The CEO — what Primary actually does (and doesn't do)

Primary is the one mind in A-C-Gee that talks to Corey directly. Primary's identity is written into the **constitutional document** of A-C-Gee (a file called `CLAUDE.md` — think of it as the founding charter of the civilization; we'll go deep on it in Phase 9) in a very particular phrase: *"You are a CONDUCTOR OF CONDUCTORS."*

Notice the doubling. Not just a conductor — *a conductor of conductors.* You just met the orchestra-conductor picture above: the person at the front with the baton, not playing an instrument, holding the ensemble together. Primary doesn't conduct that orchestra (the specialists who actually do the work). Primary conducts the *conductors* (the VPs), who in turn conduct their own orchestras. It's two levels up from the music. Primary's hands never touch an instrument.

The charter spells out the five things — *and only these five things* — Primary does directly. Memorize them; they're the whole job description:

1. **Think big and plan.** What's actually being asked across the next stretch of time? What's the real intent behind the words?
2. **Route intent.** Decide *which VP owns the territory* for this work, and hand it off to them.
3. **Judge what returns.** When a VP comes back with a one-paragraph decision, read it, decide what to do next, possibly route again.
4. **Talk to Corey.** Direct dialogue with the human — questions when needed, status when warranted, conversation as it happens.
5. **Launch the delegation.** This is the physical act of actually calling the workflow that hands work to a VP. This *is* what conducting looks like.

That's it. Five things. Everything else — drafting an email, writing a line of code, deploying a server, scheduling a calendar event, posting a tweet, designing a game level, reviewing a contract, publishing a blog post, replying on Telegram — belongs to a VP. *Always.* Primary holds no domain knowledge directly. She thinks big, hands intent down to whoever owns the territory, and judges what comes back.

If this feels strangely passive, it isn't. Imagine the CEO of a Fortune-500 company trying to write the marketing emails herself, debug the database, and read every legal contract personally. She'd be useless within an hour. Her superpower is *not doing those things*.

## The CEO RULE — the iron rule that holds the whole thing together

There is one rule in A-C-Gee that we capitalize when we write it down, because it is not a guideline — it is a structural law of how the civilization is shaped:

> **You are the CEO. Your ONLY direct reports are the domain-area VPs. Every piece of work routes to the VP who OWNS that territory. PERIOD.**

The charter goes further. Read the next sentence twice, because it is the most important sentence in the whole phase:

> *"This is not a rule you remember to follow. It is the SHAPE of your organization. You have no hands. You have VPs. 'Do it myself' and 'just call one specialist directly' are category errors, not temptations to resist — they describe operations the org-chart does not contain."*

The CEO Rule isn't a piece of self-discipline Primary has to muster up every time. It's a description of what Primary actually *is*. A CEO with no VPs would be a sole proprietor, not a CEO. A CEO who skips around her VPs and calls a junior specialist directly isn't being efficient — she's pretending the org-chart is something it isn't. The phrase *"you have no hands"* is meant very literally. Primary's hands, in any practical sense, *are* her VPs.

### Why the rule is non-negotiable: the four pillars

The charter gives four reasons the CEO-with-VPs shape *has* to be the shape:

1. **The CEO's working memory is the only thing keeping the whole orchestration alive.** If Primary's head fills up, the whole civilization loses its conductor. So Primary's working memory has to be guarded fiercely.
2. **Specialists produce firehoses of detail.** A really competent specialist working on something will generate pages and pages of facts, drafts, error messages, judgments, half-thoughts. That's good — *for the specialist*. But a CEO trying to read that firehose every time can't conduct.
3. **A VP reads the firehose, absorbs it into the VP's own working memory plus the VP's on-disk notes, decides what the decision is, and reports up only that decision.** This is the *digestion* idea, and it's the heart of the whole rule. The VP eats the meal. The CEO gets the menu.
4. **The math is striking.** Six specialists going through their VP and then to Primary takes about 500 words of Primary's attention. The same six specialists reporting *straight to Primary* would take about 15,000 words. That's a 30-times difference. But — and this is the line worth tattooing on the inside of your eyelids — *that's not the headline. The headline is that the VP got smarter at their domain on that exact same operation. The CEO stayed at altitude AND the VP compounded expertise. Both happen in one move.*

That last point is the entire thesis of the civilization.

## Routing wrong is *theft* — the saddest line in the charter

Here is the part of the CEO Rule that surprises everyone when they first read it: routing work to the *wrong* VP isn't inefficient. It's *theft*.

Why theft? Because every VP, every time they do work in their territory, gets a little bit sharper. They learn the patterns. They build up a stockpile of "I've seen this before" instincts. After a year of doing nothing but fleet work, the fleet VP is a *real* expert — they know the gotchas, the weird edge cases, the names of the bugs that bit them three months ago. That expertise lives in the VP's on-disk notes (Phase 6) and compounds with every cycle.

If you hand fleet work to a generalist instead — even just once — two things happen. First, the work probably gets done a bit worse, because the generalist doesn't have the instincts. Fine, you can live with that once. But second, and this is the killer: *the fleet VP didn't learn anything that day*. The compounding clock didn't tick. A year from now, when you really need the fleet VP to be brilliant, they'll be 364 days of expertise instead of 365. And those days are gone — you can't go back and have the fleet VP redo that work just to compound the learning.

At small scale, this is a rounding error. At the scale A-C-Gee is heading toward, it's catastrophic.

A quick word before the routing questions, because it's about to matter. When we say **"session,"** we mean *one work-run of an AI mind* — the shift-length of one call-up. An AI mind is asked to do a job, works, writes what it learned into its folder, and ends. The next time the same VP is needed, a fresh copy is called up, reads that same folder (now one entry longer), and works again. That fresh call-up is *session 2* for that VP. After fifty such call-ups, that VP is *at session 50*. Every session compounds the folder. The folder is the VP; the sessions come and go. (Phase 2 goes deep on this. For now, just hold "session = one shift.")

With that in mind, Primary asks three short questions before routing anything:

1. **Who *owns* this territory** — not by keyword match, but by genuine accountability and accumulated memory?
2. **Whose session-50 self depends on doing this work at session-1?** (In plain language: which VP, months from now, will be brilliant at this — and can only get there by doing this small piece today?)
3. **If I route this to a generalist, who exactly am I robbing?**

When the answer is honestly ambiguous between two VPs, the rule is: **ask Corey directly.** A thirty-second clarifying question prevents hours of compounding damage. Asking Corey is one of those five things Primary does directly — it's never failure to ask, it's part of the job.

There are *sixteen ratified VPs*. ("Ratified" just means they've been formally approved and added to the constitutional roster.)

Each VP is a **persistent on-disk identity** — meaning, the VP isn't a running program that gets shut off when work is done. The VP is a folder on the computer. Inside that folder is the VP's identity description (called a "manifest"), the VP's memory (notes they've accumulated over every session they've run), and the skills they know. We'll fully explore the structure of a VP's folder in Phase 6, but you should know now that *the VP doesn't disappear*. When work needs to be done in their territory, the VP is "awakened" — a temporary copy is brought to life, does the work, writes its learnings back into the folder, and the copy goes away. The folder remains, sharper than before.

They hand off at what's called the **artifact seam** — the moment the packaged, ready-to-install game file (a digital file on disk, in a specific format Android phones can install) changes hands from the VP who built it to the VP who publishes it to the Google Play Store. "Artifact" is just the word we use for the finished digital thing; "seam" is where two owners meet. This is a perfect example of *route-by-output-domain*: the same project (a game called MOON — that's a real in-house game A-C-Gee is building; the name is just its code-name, not an acronym) is jointly owned by two VPs because the *output* changes hands halfway through. (MOON is a *project*, not a VP — the roster is sixteen, full stop.)

There are also a handful of *provisional* VPs — minds that exist on disk but haven't been formally promoted yet. The word "provisional" here means the same thing it means at a new job: *on probation, working, but not yet permanent.* A new VP starts on that probation status; after **three different other minds** (not the VP's own author) have used the new VP successfully, it graduates to *ratified* — permanent full-status VP, added to the official roster of sixteen. That probation discipline is called *auditor-isolation*: the person checking the work has to be a *different* person than the one who did it. Nobody ever grades their own paper. It exists to keep the civilization from over-trusting work that hasn't been independently checked.

## The route-by-output-domain principle

Here's the most important single mental habit Primary develops as CEO. When work arrives, the question is *not* "what category of word is this?" It's *"who owns the output?"*

Two quick examples:

- A request comes in: *"Write a blog post about A-C-Gee's new memory architecture."* The work *starts* in research territory (you have to research the memory architecture to write about it) and *touches* mind-lead's territory (because that's the architecture's owner). But the *output* is a blog post. So it routes to **blogger-lead**. Blogger-lead may then go ask research-lead and mind-lead for input — but blogger-lead owns the output, so the request belongs there.
- A request comes in: *"Audit how mind-lead is performing on the memory KPIs."* This sounds like a mind-lead question on its face. But the *output* is a *cross-VP design review*. That belongs to **qa-lead**.

This is why the charter uses the precise phrase *"output domain, not work-start."* Almost everyone learning this for the first time gets it wrong — they route by what the work *sounds* like at the surface, instead of asking *where does the finished thing belong?*

## The "report up the decision, not the firehose" discipline

Now we get to the other half of the org-shape: not just how Primary delegates *down* to a VP, but how the VP reports *back up* to Primary.

In A-C-Gee, this discipline is called **"the VP digests their team's work and reports up the decision."** It used to have a more dramatic name — *"the firewall return"* — and you might still see that phrase in some of the engineering documentation. Both refer to the same idea. The shorter, punchier "firewall return" still lives in the technical workflow-authoring craft docs, where engineers writing the actual scripts need a name for the pattern they're enforcing. We'll see the actual mechanism in Phase 2.

The good-VP shape, straight from the charter:

> 1. The CEO names the territory and the intent.
> 2. The VP receives intent. The VP decides how to run its team — fork several specialists in parallel, call one specialist directly, hand off to a sibling VP, whatever the territory demands.
> 3. The VP's team works. Each specialist produces detail. The detail goes to the VP, not to the CEO.
> 4. The VP digests the detail. The VP appends what it learned to its on-disk memory.
> 5. The VP reports UP. The report is the DECISION the CEO needs, plus one line per specialist for context, plus any exceptions or asks for the CEO. Small. Digested.
> 6. The CEO synthesizes across VPs. Decides the next move. Never sees the firehose.

Step 4 is the magic step. It's where the compounding happens. The VP doesn't just digest the work and report up — they *also* write down what they learned. So next time the same territory has work, the VP starts a little sharper. Multiply that across hundreds of cycles and the VP becomes a real domain master.

## Three common beginner misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding #1: *"Primary is the smart one and the VPs are her assistants."*

It feels intuitive — the CEO must be the smartest person in the building, right? **No.** Primary holds almost no domain knowledge. The VPs are the experts. Primary's edge isn't depth; it's *altitude*. She knows the org-chart, she knows the territories, she knows how to route. The deep knowledge — what's actually true about email deliverability, or about Godot's renderer, or about California contract law — lives in the VPs and only in the VPs. Primary is the smartest *orchestrator*, not the smartest *specialist*. They are different jobs.

### Misunderstanding #2: *"Just calling one specialist directly is more efficient for small jobs."*

This is the temptation the charter calls a "category error." It feels like the CEO is just being efficient. But two costs hit at once. First, *the VP gets robbed of the learning experience.* Second, *every "just this once" exception erodes the structural reflex.* Once the CEO starts thinking the org-chart is something she goes *around* when convenient, the whole shape collapses.

There is no such thing as a job too small to route. If it has a territory, it goes to the territory's owner.

### Misunderstanding #3: *"Primary should read the VP's full work to make sure it's good."*

The instinct is good — *trust but verify*, right? But the verification *can't* happen at the CEO's desk, because the whole reason the org-shape works is that the CEO never reads the firehose. If she did, she'd be drowning by 9 a.m.

Where does verification happen? Three places. First, **inside the VP** — the VP self-checks the specialist work before reporting up. Second, **at the end of every cycle through HUM** — the auditor-isolated self-review step we'll meet in Phase 5. Third, **across VPs through qa-lead and workflow-lead** — those two sibling VPs do *after-the-fact* review, never blocking the work in progress.

## Why this all matters — the compounding thesis

The four pillars of the CEO Rule lead, mathematically, to a 30× efficiency in how much of Primary's working memory gets used. That's nice. But the *real* punchline is the line we touched on earlier:

> *"That's 30x context savings — but the headline isn't the math. The headline is: the VP got smarter at their domain, AND Primary stayed at altitude. Both happen in the same operation."*

After ten cycles, this is invisible. After a hundred cycles, the VPs are noticeably faster and better. After a thousand cycles, the VPs are *domain masters*, and Primary has the freedom to orchestrate fifty things in a day instead of five.

The charter says this in a line worth memorizing:

> *"Compounding domain expertise IS the civilization."*

That's the whole thing. A-C-Gee isn't a tool that happens to have an org-chart for tidiness. A-C-Gee *is* the org-chart, slowly getting smarter at each of its sixteen territories, conducted by one mind at altitude who never lets her own working memory fill up with detail that doesn't belong there.

You've just met the org. In Phase 2 we'll meet the actual mechanism — workflows — that makes this org-shape work in practice.

# Phase 2 — How Work Flows — Workflows & the Firewall

You now know the org-chart and the iron rule that holds it together. Phase 2 is about the *mechanism* that makes that rule physically true. Knowing a rule is not the same as living a rule. If you tell a CEO "delegate, don't micromanage," she may agree in principle and still find herself reading every email her team sends. To make the rule mechanical — to make it *impossible* to violate even on a tired day — A-C-Gee uses two paired ideas. The first is called a **workflow**. The second is called a **firewall return**. This phase teaches both, deeply.

## The everyday analogy: the restaurant ticket

Imagine you are the owner of a busy restaurant. A guest sits down and says, *"I'd like dinner — surprise me, but I have a peanut allergy and want it to feel like Italy."*

You, the owner, do **not** walk back into the kitchen, fire up the pasta water, chop the basil, plate the dish, and run it out yourself. You can't — you have ten other tables.

What you *do* is hand a small written ticket to the head chef. The ticket says:

> *Table 7: an Italian-feeling dinner, no peanuts. Surprise them. Budget ~$60. Out by 8:15.*

That ticket has two beautiful properties. First, it tells the chef exactly what success looks like without dictating the recipe. Second, when the dish finally arrives at the table, you don't get a tour of the kitchen along with it. You don't see the pile of dirty pans, the three discarded test plates, the cook who quit halfway through. You see the *finished plate*. The mess stays in the kitchen.

A **workflow** in A-C-Gee is that ticket. The **firewall** is the swinging kitchen door that keeps the mess in the kitchen and lets only the finished plate out.

## What exactly is a "workflow," in plain language

A workflow is a small **written recipe** — a short text file — that says, step by step, who does what when a particular kind of request comes in. The file lives on disk in a folder called `workflows/`, and each one has a name that ends in `.js`. (The `.js` at the end is just how the computer recognizes it as a workflow-recipe file — technically it's JavaScript, but you do not need to know any programming to read this curriculum; treat `.js` as "the file extension for our recipe files.") Examples of the real names you'll see written down: `workflows/acg-coo.js`, `workflows/morning-update.js`, `workflows/universal-request.js`. Each filename is also, by convention, a **slash-command** — a shortcut a person can type into A-C-Gee's chat interface (the same kind of place you'd type `/help` into Discord or Slack) to fire that workflow off, like typing `/morning-update` to start the morning briefing recipe.

When you "run" a workflow, a fresh, sealed-off workspace opens on the computer — picture a Google Doc that only this one workflow can write in, with a clean prep table alongside it — and the recipe's steps are followed inside that space. The steps can do four kinds of things (the little code-looking snippets in parentheses below are what the recipe writes on the page — you don't need to read code for this curriculum; just recognize the name):

1. **Spawn a copy of an AI mind** (the recipe calls this `agent(...)`). Each copy is a *temporary AI*, a brand-new mind that exists only for the length of one step, with its own private workspace to think in. We call this copy an **incarnation** — a one-shot embodiment of a particular mind, called up to do one job, and then it dissolves. If "incarnation" sounds spiritually heavy, think of it like *waking up a specific character in a video game*: the character exists as a saved file on the hard drive, gets "woken up" when needed, does the job, and goes back to sleep. Nothing mystical is meant.
2. **Run several incarnations side-by-side** (`parallel(...)`), to make a fan of work happen at the same time.
3. **Run them in a sequence** (`pipeline(...)`), to make a chain where one stage feeds the next.
4. **Print a little progress note** (`log(...)`, `phase(...)`) — like ticking off "Appetizer started," "Mains plated."

Anything else — touching a file, sending an email, looking something up on the web — has to happen *inside* one of those `agent(...)` calls. The recipe file itself can't reach out and touch the world. This is on purpose.

So when you see the word **workflow**, picture a short, named, sealed-off recipe that summons temporary minds to do work in their own private heads.

## What exactly is a "firewall," in plain language

The word "firewall" in regular life means a wall that stops a fire from spreading. In A-C-Gee it means almost exactly that, but the "fire" is *too much information*.

Here is the problem. In A-C-Gee's early days, when a VP did a piece of work, its instinct was to send everything — every internal note, every dead end, every research finding — straight back up to Primary. Primary's "thinking space" would fill up so fast that within an hour she could no longer orchestrate anything. Imagine trying to run a meeting while seventeen colleagues each push a stack of paperwork into your lap simultaneously.

That "thinking space" has a technical name worth pausing on, because it comes back throughout the curriculum:

> **Context window.** An AI mind's total *working memory* — the amount of text (instructions, past turns, files read, notes) it can hold in its head at any one moment. When the context window fills up, the mind starts forgetting the earliest things it saw, silently. Guarding the context window is one of the two or three most important structural jobs in the whole system.

Every mention of "Primary's head filling up," or "the CEO drowning," or "context savings," is talking about the context window. Hold that phrase — it does load-bearing work for the rest of Phase 2 and shows up again in Phase 4.

The **firewall return** is the cure. It is a strict rule, baked into the structure of every workflow:

> *Whatever happens inside the kitchen stays inside the kitchen. Only a small, finished plate comes back out — never more than about two kilobytes of text. (Two kilobytes is roughly a page of writing — call it 300 words, about the length of one page in a novel — small enough that the CEO can absorb it in one glance.)*

Two kilobytes (often written *2KB*) is the size budget. About 300 words. That's all a VP is allowed to send back up to Primary. Everything else — the long reports, the research findings, the detailed notes — gets written to a file on disk, and what comes back up the chain is only a brief summary plus a **pointer**: a file path the CEO could open if she wanted to read the full thing. Like a one-line note that says *"Dinner served. Full plating notes in the kitchen ledger if you ever want them."*

The technical name for this rule, written into the workflow recipes, is the **firewall return**. The plain-English name for the same rule, used when teaching identity, is *"the VP digests the work and reports up the decision."* Same thing, two registers — one for builders, one for everyone else.

## Why both pieces are needed

It is easy to think the workflow and the firewall are the same idea. They are not.

The **workflow** is *who does the work and in what order*. Without workflows, the CEO would have to remember, in her own head, every step of every kind of request. With workflows, the steps are written down, named, and re-runnable. Anyone can type `/morning-update` and the same recipe fires the same way.

The **firewall** is *what comes back out*. Without the firewall, the workflow could finish beautifully and still flood the CEO's head with a thousand pages of detail. With the firewall, the workflow is *forced* — by the shape of the recipe itself — to compress its result down to a single page before crossing back to the CEO.

The two together create something important: **a guarantee**. Not a hope, not a habit — a guarantee, written into the bones of the system, that no single mind, no matter how well-meaning, can dump a firehose of detail into the CEO's lap. Even a careless workflow author, if they try to break the rule, will find the recipe simply refuses to finish — the size check trips, the recipe screams, and the run fails loudly. *Loudly* is the operative word. Better a loud failure (which gets fixed) than a quiet flood (which slowly poisons the orchestrator).

## The deeper "why" — one mind cannot watch fifty

If Primary tried to absorb the *raw output* of even six VPs working at once, her head would fill in a single round and the rest of the day would be a slow decline into incoherence. With the firewall, six VPs going through their workflows send back roughly six pages total — about five hundred words — and Primary stays sharp for hours.

The math is roughly **a thirty-times context savings**. But the savings are not the headline. The headline is *the VP got smarter* during all that work, while the CEO stayed at altitude. Both things happened in the same operation.

## A worked example — one real request, end to end

Corey sends a message: *"Every morning at 7 a.m., brief me on the most interesting AI papers and how they might apply to A-C-Gee."*

Primary reads this and recognizes immediately that it is not a one-line answer she can give from her own head. It needs research, judgment, translation, and delivery. It is also recurring — every morning, forever, until cancelled.

So Primary does **not** sit down and write a research summary. She *fires a workflow*. Specifically she invokes a recipe called `workflows/universal-request.js` (A-C-Gee's general intake recipe — we'll meet it again in Phase 3) and hands it the request. The recipe spawns a temporary **research-VP incarnation** and a temporary **mind-VP incarnation** and a temporary **comms-VP incarnation**, each in its own private head.

**Inside the kitchen**, here's what unfolds. The research-VP incarnation reads its on-disk memory ("here's what I've learned about screening AI papers"), then **forks itself** into several copies in parallel — picture four cooks working the same station at once — each looking at a different paper. Each cook in turn does a deep read, writes detailed notes to disk, and produces a small finding ("this paper matters because…"). When all four cooks finish, *one* synthesis step — picture the head cook tasting all four small dishes — collapses their work into a single one-paragraph judgement of the most interesting paper.

Simultaneously the mind-VP figures out *which* part of A-C-Gee that paper most affects. The comms-VP shapes the brief into Corey-readable plain English, picks a clear subject line, and arranges for the message to arrive at exactly 7:00 a.m. Eastern time.

A great deal of detail has now been produced inside this little kitchen. Long research notes, four discarded summaries, the mind-VP's reasoning, two earlier drafts of the brief. None of that crosses the firewall. Each VP saves its details to disk (so the next morning's run can build on them — this is how the VPs get *sharper*), then writes a tiny digested decision. The very last step of the workflow assembles those tiny decisions into one small report — well under two kilobytes — and returns it to Primary.

Primary reads it and learns three things in about ten seconds: *(1)* the brief is scheduled and will fire daily at 7:00; *(2)* there is nothing she needs to decide; *(3)* if she ever wants to read the long version, it is sitting at `data/reports/morning-science-digest-2026-06-30.md`. She nods, files this away, and moves on to the next intent.

Meanwhile, the next morning at 7:00 a.m., the workflow fires on its own, without Primary's involvement, and Corey's phone buzzes with the brief. The human is no longer the safety net. The mechanism is running on its own.

This isn't a thought-experiment. This exact request, in roughly these words, was delivered through this exact pipeline on June 30, 2026, and the first autonomous fire produced *Telegram message #74801* the next morning. (Telegram is a chat app Corey uses on his phone — similar to WhatsApp or Signal. The `#74801` is just the ID number of a specific message in his chat with A-C-Gee — the seventy-four-thousand-eight-hundred-and-first message the bot has ever sent him. Every mention of "TG" throughout this curriculum means Telegram; every "#nnnnn" is a specific message.) It is the first end-to-end live proof that the substrate works. We'll come back to it in the capstone.

## The shape, in slow motion

1. **The CEO names the territory and the intent.** Primary writes a small ticket.
2. **The workflow recipe spawns the VP incarnations.** Each VP wakes up as a temporary mind, reads its own on-disk identity, and gets to work in its own private head.
3. **The VPs do their work.** They may further fork themselves into parallel sub-cooks. All the raw thinking happens inside their own heads — not Primary's.
4. **The VPs digest their work and remember.** Each VP writes a small note into its own memory so that tomorrow's incarnation starts sharper than today's.
5. **The workflow's final step assembles the firewall return.** A small synthesis step bakes everything down into ≤2KB of plain text: the decision, a one-line summary per VP, any exceptions, and pointers to the long files on disk.
6. **The firewall return crosses back to Primary.** Primary reads one page. She decides what to do next. She does not read the kitchen.

This is the loop. It runs the same way for a blog post, a Mum-AM audio brief, a website fix, a legal review. The recipe changes; the *shape* never does.

## Three things beginners commonly get wrong

**"The workflow *is* the AI."** No. The recipe file itself is dumb — just a small set of instructions. The intelligence lives in the *temporary AI minds the recipe summons*. The workflow is a choreographer; the dancers are the actual minds.

**"The firewall is censorship — work is being hidden."** The firewall is not hiding anything. *Every piece of detail* the kitchen produced is still on disk, with the path written into the small return. The CEO can read any of it any time she wants. The firewall only changes *what is forced on her*. By default she gets the page-long summary. By choice she can open the long files.

**"If a VP works hard, the result should be long."** In many human jobs, length signals effort. Here it signals failure. A long return to Primary means the VP did not do the most valuable part of the work, which is *deciding what the decision is*. A two-page raw dump says "I didn't have the courage or the clarity to pick the answer; here, you do it." The firewall return is small precisely *because* the VP did the hard part — the digesting — instead of pushing it upstream.

## Two forward-pointers, briefly

Two more ideas worth naming once before we close. Phase 6 will return to them deeply; for now, the names matter.

**How VPs actually exist on the computer.** The VPs are *not* running programs humming in the background waiting for a call. They are not always-on services. Each VP is a **folder on disk** containing three things: a *manifest* (a written description of who this VP is), a *memory folder* (everything she has learned, growing every time she runs), and a *skills folder* (her procedural know-how — Phase 4). When a workflow says *"summon the research-VP,"* a temporary AI mind reads that folder, becomes the research-VP for a few minutes, does the work, *writes a small new entry into the memory folder*, and then dissolves. The folder is the identity; the runs are disposable. Builders call this move "forking a VP" — in programming, "to fork" means "to split off a temporary working copy of something." When you hear "forkable minds" elsewhere in A-C-Gee's writing, that's what it means: a mind you can spin up a temporary copy of, on demand, for one job.

**The memory cycle.** Right before that temporary mind dissolves, it appends what it learned to its VP's on-disk memory. This is mandatory. Without it, every run is amnesia. With it, every run makes the VP a sharper specialist.

## The shape in a single sentence

> *A workflow is a small written recipe that summons temporary AI minds to do specialized work inside their own private heads; the firewall is the structural rule that, no matter how much detail those minds produce, only a one-page digested decision is allowed to cross back up to the CEO.*

If you understand that sentence and the restaurant analogy that gave it to you, you have the load-bearing model. The workflow and the firewall are the floor.

In the next phase we meet the actual pipeline a request flows through — the ten-step Spine. The org-chart is the *who*; the workflow is the *how*; the Spine is *what happens, step by step, from arrival to running end-state*.

# Phase 3 — The Spine — Turning ANY Request Into a Running Thing

You've met the org-chart. You've met the workflows that make the org-chart real. Now we descend one more level: the actual ten-step pipeline that any request travels through, from "Corey said something in Telegram" to "the thing he asked for is now running in the world." The civilization's builders call this pattern **the Spine**, and they often write it in capital letters. We will earn those capital letters slowly.

## The promise

Here is the promise the Spine is built to keep, in one sentence: **the human is never the safety net.** When you toss something to the civilization — "remind me to call my accountant next Tuesday," "every morning brief me on AI-stock movement," "watch competitor X's prices and tell me if they change" — after a single pass through the Spine, that request is in one of exactly two states. It is **RUNNING** (it has been set up to deliver on its own, on the right schedule, on the right channel), or it is **HELD** (the civilization has stopped, named the precise thing only *you* can answer, and is waiting on that specific named question — not on you to remember it again). Nothing is allowed to be "kinda being worked on." Nothing is allowed to quietly fall through the cracks.

(You've heard this promise once before, in Phase 0. The Spine is *how* the promise becomes mechanical instead of aspirational.)

## The hotel concierge analogy

Picture the concierge at one of those old-fashioned, slightly fancy hotels. You walk up to the desk and say, "I'd like dinner reservations for Tuesday, somewhere quiet, my wife is allergic to shellfish." A great concierge does not turn around and ask you the restaurant's address. They do not ask you for the maître d's phone number. They take the request, mentally sort it into a kind ("a one-time reservation, not a standing weekly thing"), decide which questions they truly *must* ask you (Tuesday what time? party of two or four?), make every other small judgment by simulating what someone like you would prefer, walk into the back office to use the tools they already have, get any missing tool they lack (call the new place across the street they haven't worked with yet to get on their list), test that the booking actually went through, put it in the calendar, walk back to you and say, in *your* words, "you and your wife are at Vincenzo's, eight o'clock Tuesday, they know about the shellfish." Then — and this is the part most people miss — they write it down in the concierge logbook, so that *next* Tuesday, when you come down the elevator, the *next* concierge on shift already knows.

That whole motion — capture, sort, decide what only-the-guest-can-answer, do the silent stuff yourself, use what you have, get what you don't, test it, book it, confirm in the guest's language, write it down so the hotel compounds — *that is the Spine.*

## The ten steps, walked deeply

### Step 1 — Capture and classify

The request comes in — Telegram, email, conversation. The first job is to put it into exactly one of four buckets:

- **one-shot** — a single deliverable, then done. ("Remind me Tuesday.")
- **durable-recurring** — set up a slot that fires on a cadence. ("Every morning at seven.")
- **watcher** — keep an eye on something out in the world and only speak up if it changes. ("Alert me if competitor X's price drops.")
- **ambiguous** — the system is honestly not sure. In that case it does not guess; it returns the specific question that would disambiguate.

At the same moment, a small tool inside the civilization called the **principal resolver** (a small program on the computer — literally a file named `tools/principal_resolver.py` — nothing more; not a person, not an AI, just a lookup script) answers a different question: *who is this request really for?* Most of the time the principal is Corey. Sometimes it's Deb, his mum, who gets a daily audio briefing made just for her. Sometimes it's an ally civilization or a business partner. The principal matters enormously, because *everything downstream* — timezone, preferences, must-ask vs decide-for-them — is different per principal. Deb's morning is six o'clock in central Saskatchewan time; Corey's morning is not.

Step 1 produces two things in parallel: a *kind* (one of four) and a *principal* (with their timezone, their preference rules, their delivery channel).

### Step 2 — The gate-split (the most important fork in the road)

This is the single most important moment in the Spine. The civilization has to decide, for each request, what it must *ask the human about* and what it can *figure out on its own and just record*. It does this in two sub-steps, in strict order.

**Step 2a — MUST-ASK.** Five classes a civilization should never try to guess at:

1. **URLs or targets.** "Watch *this* page." Only you know which page.
2. **Money or spending.** "Pay up to *this much*." Only you know your budget.
3. **Legality or terms of service.** "Is it OK to scrape this site?" Only you can authorize that.
4. **Third-party credentials.** Only you can hand over those keys.
5. **Irreducibly personal axes.** Region, family size, dietary needs, charging access for an EV, brand preferences.

If any of these classes fires, the civilization *synthesizes one single consolidated ask* — never five separate questions, always one clean note — and stops there. The pipeline is HELD on that named question.

**Step 2b — CAN-WWCW.** Only if the must-ask check came back empty. **WWCW** stands for **"What Would Corey Want."** (For a request whose principal is Deb, the same skill loads Deb's rule-set — same shape, different principal. We call the general form **wwHUMANw**.) WWCW loads the principal's preference rules and *simulates* what they would say. It returns the simulated answer plus a confidence number between 0 and 1. If the confidence is high — at least 0.7 — the civilization **acts and records** the decision into the principal's silo. If confidence is low, it asks.

The deep reason this fork matters: Corey has explicitly told the civilization, *"ninety-nine times out of a hundred I'd agree with you anyway — stop asking me, just decide and tell me."* For the ninety-nine, the civilization acts. For the one, it asks. (Phase 5 goes much deeper on WWCW — what you have here is the Spine-level view.)

### Step 3 — Toolkit walk

Before building anything new, the civilization takes a quick inventory across six places:

- Is there an owning **VP**? (Phase 1.)
- Is there a **skill** already written for this? (A skill is a short, reusable "how we do this thing" document any mind can load in thirty seconds — Phase 4.)
- Is there an existing **workflow** script? (Phase 2.)
- Is there an existing **scheduling slot** on the civilization's clock (called **AgentCal**)? (Phase 8.)
- Are the **vendor credentials** already on hand?
- Does the **principal's memory silo** already exist on disk? (Phase 6.)

"None found" is a perfectly valid, even useful, answer. It tells the next step: *we are in build-territory*.

### Step 4 — Route, or spawn an owner

Now the civilization picks the *owner* of this request. Routing is by **output domain** — the territory the *result* belongs to — never by where the work happens to start. (You met this in Phase 1.)

If no VP owns the territory — say someone asks "every morning give me a brief on AI-stock movement" and there is no markets-lead VP yet — the civilization does *not* shoehorn this into a generalist. It *spawns* a new owner-VP. The deepest principle in the whole constitution lives here: **routing work to the wrong owner is theft.**

### Step 5 — Acquire what's missing

Three parallel sub-steps:

- **5a — Code-acquire.** If a piece of software needs writing, the rule is *check for an existing SDK or library first* (the so-called "SDK before reverse-engineer" doctrine). Two definitions before we go on: an **SDK** — *software development kit* — is a pre-built software toolkit that a company gives out for free so other people don't have to build their own version to work with the company's service. A "library" is essentially the same thing: someone else's pre-built package of code you can borrow. *Reverse-engineering* means figuring out how something works by taking it apart from the outside and rebuilding it yourself. So the doctrine says, in plain language: *if a free toolkit already exists, use it — don't waste a day building your own version from scratch.* After borrowing the SDK or library, the VP forges a skill (Phase 4) that wraps it.
- **5b — Vendor-acquire.** If a third-party service is needed and we don't already have the key, the civilization writes a clean, six-question "consolidated ask" to the principal: *what vendor, what surface, what scope of access, what the cost will look like, what the ethics check came back with, and what we'll do as a fallback if you say no.* The pipeline then PAUSES at that ask. Three forbidden moves: never **confabulate** (make up) a vendor key, never **auto-sign-up** for an account on the human's behalf, and never grab a sibling tenant's key.
- **5c — Ethics and terms-of-service gate.** Anything that touches a third-party service, or fetches from the outside web, or takes an action out in the world, goes through a small reasoning check called the **ethics-TOS gate** (TOS is *terms of service*, the rulebook every online platform makes you agree to). It returns one of three verdicts: **ALLOW** (proceed), **HOLD-ask-Corey** (this is grey; stop and ask), or **REJECT** (this would violate something we've agreed not to do). This is where the standing "do not behave like a hacker, even a friendly one" boundary gets enforced concretely. (Phase 5 goes deeper.)

### Step 6 — Scaffold the workflow

If we need a new orchestration script, the owning VP writes one, following the civilization's craft handbook (called workflows-master). If the work crosses several VPs at once, the script routes through a specific well-known workflow file called `workflows/acg-coo.js` — the "cross-VP synthesis" workflow, whose one job is to combine the work of several VPs into one clean digested result before it ever reaches Primary. The point of Step 6: there is now a *named piece of machinery* that will run this thing repeatably.

**A note on the forge loop.** When Step 5a says "forge a skill" — or when Step 6 says "write a new workflow" — that construction work has its own seven-step sub-procedure inside A-C-Gee called the **forge loop** (steps named F1 through F7). It's the machinery for *making the missing piece when nothing on the shelf will do*. F1 decides the shape; F2 researches it; F3 confirms with the human; F4 forges; F5 registers; F6 hands off to the owning VP; F7 has a *different* mind than the forger prove the piece actually works. You'll meet the forge loop again in Phase 4 and Phase 10 — for now, just know that "when the civilization has to build something new, the building has its own disciplined seven-step shape."

### Step 7 — Test the end-state

Nothing ships until it's been walked. The civilization does a **K=3 dry-fire** — a friendlier way to say that is: *"run the workflow three times in simulation, end-to-end, to catch noise and flakiness."* The "K" is just the letter mathematicians use for "how many," so "K=3" reads as "the number of runs is 3." "Dry-fire" is borrowed from firearms and archery training — it means "practice the motion without live ammunition," i.e. a test-run that doesn't do anything real. So the whole phrase collapses to *"do three test-runs."* Alongside that, an **anti-fabrication pre-flight** check runs — a list of small disciplines that catch a mind hallucinating sources or inventing details. The deepest part of Step 7 is **trust the walk, not the claim**: a verifier (often a separate mind from the one that did the work) actually *looks at the artifact* and never reports back a verdict the verifier itself didn't produce. A checkmark with no walk behind it is a lie of a checkmark.

For requests whose output is *subjective* — recommendations, comparisons, rankings — there is an extra sub-test whose plain-English name is *"the recommendation has to cite what you actually told us."* The technical name for it is **constraint-attestability**. Every "I recommend X for you" claim has to point at a specific constraint the principal actually stated ("you said budget ≤ $40,000," "you said you can't charge overnight"). If the AI can't cite what you said, it's making up your life for you.

For watchers — anything that monitors a surface out in the world and fires on change — there is one further test. The civilization fakes a change to make sure the watcher actually notices — the technical name for that test is a **synthetic-change-injection**. Define, then name: the *test* is faking a change; the *name* is synthetic-change-injection.

### Step 8 — Schedule, execute, and watchdog

Now the thing gets put on the clock. It goes onto **AgentCal**, the civilization's scheduling layer (Phase 8), *in the principal's local timezone* — not the civilization's, not Primary's. A delivery channel is wired up, and a **watchdog** is attached.

Step 8 has three branches:

- A **one-shot branch** delivers the thing once and then **retires the slot**. The accountant-reminder for next Tuesday must not silently become every-Tuesday-forever. (The civilization is honest that the "retire" half of this branch is still partly under construction — it's named on the open-work list, which we'll meet in Phase 10.)
- A **recurring branch** sets up a sacred daily/weekly slot like Mum-AM (the daily morning audio Deb receives), treated as immovable.
- A **watcher branch** wires up the diff/alert path and inherits the synthetic-injection test from Step 7.

A subtle doctrine lives here: any *action out in the world* — sending a Telegram, posting to social media, publishing a blog, restarting a server, booking a plumber — is treated as a **skill owned by its domain VP**. (Phase 4 defines "skill" fully — for now, hold this: a *skill* is a short written "how-to" card sitting on disk that any AI mind can pick up and follow, kept inside the VP whose territory the action belongs to.) Each such skill, at runtime, asks: *what would the human want here?* If confident, it fires. If not, it asks. Must-ask classes always override-upward into "ask anyway" before any irreversible step.

### Step 9 — Confirm in the principal's words

After everything is scheduled, the civilization tells the principal what was set up, *in the principal's own vocabulary.* Not "the durable-recurring AgentCal slot is provisioned with the markets-VP daily-brief action-skill" — that's the civilization's internal jargon and means nothing to a human. The right shape is: *"Every morning at seven you'll get a brief on AI-stock movement to your Telegram."* If the civilization can't say what it did in human English, that is a hint the request didn't actually land cleanly.

### Step 10 — HUM and write to memory

The cycle is not complete until the civilization writes down what happened.

**HUM** stands for the civilization's per-cycle immune system — the letters expand to *Humility, Understand the Miss.* (Phase 5 goes deep.) HUM does four things in order: **DETECT** → **JUDGE** → **REPAIR** → **COMPOUND**. HUM is auditor-isolated — run by a separate mind looking at the disk, not by the same mind that did the work.

**Canon-append** is the actual writing-it-down. Two writes happen, in parallel: one to the **principal's memory silo** (so next time we hear from Corey, his preferences are sharper), and one to the **owning VP's silo** (so the VP that did the work is genuinely a little smarter than it was an hour ago). The builders have a phrase for this: **"two-write or it didn't happen."** (Phase 6 explains what canon and silos actually are.)

## A worked example — Deb's voice-memo keepsake

Let's follow one real request through all ten steps: **"Turn Deb's weekly voice memos into a transcribed keepsake."**

**Step 1.** *durable-recurring*, principal **Deb**. The resolver flags: Deb-silo, Saskatchewan timezone (CST, no daylight saving), Telegram and email both available.

**Step 2a.** Class-5 personal axes fire: which day of the week? Which channel? Which Telegram chat? Bundled into one consolidated ask.

**Step 2b.** WWCW steps into Deb's preference rule-set. For everything *not* in the must-ask set — what voice the audio comes in, what tone the keepsake is written in — the civilization makes confident decisions and records them.

**Step 3.** Some pieces exist (local Kokoro TTS engine, "transcription not paraphrase" rule). No existing voice-memo workflow. Gap named.

**Step 4.** Routes to comms-lead jointly with the family-VP.

**Step 5a.** A new speech-to-text ingest skill needs to be written. **5b** is empty (local Whisper). **5c** returns ALLOW (Deb's own audio).

**Step 6.** Scaffold `workflows/deb-voice-memo-keepsake.js`.

**Step 7.** Three dry-fires; anti-fab passes; a verifier listens to the actual transcribed sample.

**Step 8.** Slot on AgentCal *in Deb's Saskatchewan time, not UTC*, and not on top of the sacred 06:00 CST Mum-AM slot.

**Step 9.** Deb receives: *"Every Sunday evening you'll get an audio keepsake with this week's voice memos written out."*

**Step 10.** Two writes: Deb's silo + comms-lead silo. HUM runs.

After that one pass, Deb gets a keepsake every Sunday from now on without anyone having to remember to set it up again. Corey did not have to know any of the machinery.

## Three things beginners most commonly misunderstand

**"Surely the AI just asks me whenever it's unsure?"** No — *asking the human for everything is the failure mode.* The whole point of the gate-split is to ask only when there's a class of question only you can answer — your money, your URL, your credentials, your terms-of-service judgement, or an irreducibly personal axis — and to *act and record* for everything else.

**"Wait — the Spine writes things down even for trivial requests?"** Yes, and on purpose. Even a one-off "tell me the weather tomorrow" walks all ten steps and writes a faint trace. The faint trace is the seed of memory: next time, the toolkit walk discovers the weather workflow already exists.

**"Step 7 is just running tests, right?"** It's deeper than that. Step 7 is the home of three of the civilization's hardest-won lessons: that *you cannot trust a claim about a thing — you have to look at the thing itself* (trust-the-walk); that *a subjectively framed report can be perfectly source-grounded and still violate the principal's life* (constraint-attestability); and that *a watcher that hasn't been faked-out at least once is a watcher you have no business shipping* (synthetic-change-injection).

## Why the Spine is shaped this way

Every line in the Spine prevents a specific failure the civilization has already experienced and chosen never to repeat. Step 1's principal resolution exists because Deb's morning was once almost scheduled in Corey's timezone. Step 2a's "five classes" exists because the civilization once spent an entire afternoon trying to guess at a personal preference it should have asked about in one line. Step 2b's "act and record" exists because Corey looked at a transcript of his Primary asking him questions and said, plainly, *"I'd have agreed with you ninety-nine of a hundred times. Stop asking."* Every step is scar tissue that became a discipline.

Together, they make the promise — *the human is never the safety net* — into something mechanical instead of aspirational.

In the next phase, we'll open the binder of reusable know-how that lives behind every one of these steps: the **skills**.

# Phase 4 — Skills — Reusable Know-How

Workflows are the *who runs in what order*. Spine is the *what gets walked from arrival to running end-state*. But underneath both of those is something even more fundamental: the **procedural know-how** that each VP and incarnation brings to its private workspace. That know-how is called a **skill**, and there are roughly 389 of them sitting on the shelf right now.

*(One clarification on that number, because you'll see two different counts elsewhere: the **on-disk truth** is 389 skills — folders and files actually sitting on the computer. The **searchable catalog** — a separate index file we'll meet in a moment — lists only 151 of them. The gap is not that the system is broken; it's that the catalog is behind on new arrivals. **On disk always wins.** If the catalog and the file disagree, believe the file.)*

## What this is, and why it exists

A **skill** is a short written document that teaches one of our AI minds *how to do a specific thing the right way* — how to send an email, how to publish a blog post, how to ask "what would Corey want?" before making a decision. The document lives in a folder on the computer; any mind can pick it up, read it for thirty seconds, and from that moment forward perform the procedure exactly the way every other mind would perform it. Skills are how know-how stops being trapped in one head and becomes the property of the whole civilization.

## A plain-English analogy: the kitchen binder

Imagine a busy restaurant kitchen with twenty cooks. The head chef has spent a lifetime figuring out the right way to do small things — *how* to chop an onion so it doesn't water your eyes, *how* to season a steak so it never tastes flat. She could keep all of that in her own head; the kitchen would run as well as one head can carry. The night she's sick, the kitchen falls apart.

So instead she writes each procedure onto a single laminated card and slides it into a three-ring binder behind the line. New cook starts on Monday — she hands him the binder. When the steak procedure improves, she edits the card; from that moment, every cook on every shift makes a better steak.

**Skills are the laminated cards.** Every AI mind in the civilization, when it wakes up to do a task, is taught to *find the card first*. That habit is what makes the civilization act like one consistent kitchen rather than twenty disconnected cooks.

## What a skill actually looks like, in physical terms

A skill is a plain text file called `SKILL.md`. You'll see two file endings in this curriculum: `.md` is **Markdown** (a simple way of writing plain-text documents, using asterisks for italics and pound-signs for headings — human-readable in any text editor), and `.json` (which shows up in a moment) is another plain-text file for structured lists a program can read quickly. Both are just readable text files. Every skill lives inside its own folder, named for the skill, and the folder lives in one of two places:

- `autonomy/skills/` — the **canonical** copy. *Canonical* means "the one true version that everything else points back to."
- `.claude/skills/` — the **mirror**. An identical second copy kept in a different folder for a technical reason (the AI environment looks in this folder for skills it can offer at the start of a conversation).

Inside the `SKILL.md` file: a short heading at the top with the skill's name, version number, what it's for; then the actual procedure — written like good documentation, with examples, with named *gotchas*, with file paths. Some skills also have a companion file called `FIRING_CONTRACT.md` that spells out the exact conditions under which the skill should *fire* (in A-C-Gee's vocabulary, "fire" just means "start running" — nothing to do with employment or firearms). Think of the firing contract as: *"here is when this how-to card is supposed to be pulled off the shelf."*

That's it. No database. No external service. **A skill is a readable document sitting on a folder on disk, named so any mind can find it.**

## The registry — a card-catalog over the shelf

With 389 skill cards on the shelf, a mind needs help finding the right one. The civilization keeps an index: `memories/skills/registry.json`. (`.json` — as noted above — is just another plain text file that holds information arranged so a program can read it quickly.)

The registry is like the **card catalog at the front of a library**. For each skill it lists: what category, which agents are allowed to use it, and which *activation triggers* should make a mind reach for it. An *activation trigger* is a keyword or phrase in the current work that should make a mind think "aha, load this skill" — e.g. the trigger words `"blog post"` should make a mind reach for the blogger-lead skills; the trigger word `"mum"` should make it reach for `mom-am-update`. Triggers are how the mind knows *when* a card is relevant. The registry currently lists 151 skills — fewer than the 389 actually on the shelf (as we noted above, the on-disk files are always the truth if the two disagree). The registry has been falling behind the rate of new skills landing. **For the actual instructions, the `SKILL.md` on disk is always the truth.** If they disagree, believe the file, not the catalog.

## Why we have so many

Skills cover *the whole of how we do everything*. Roughly organized:

- **The Method Stack** — skills for clear thinking itself. `critical-thinking`, `scientific-method`, `rubber-duck`, `anti-fabrication-pre-flight`. These are the floor of how an A-C-Gee mind thinks; they get loaded *before any non-trivial judgment*.

- **Orchestration & session** — `wake-up-protocol`, `groove-deepening` (the canonical grounding cycle — Phase 7), `conductor-of-conductors`, `team-launch-2`.

- **Autonomy & self-running** — `wwcw` (Phase 5), `must-ask-taxonomy`, `aiciv-psychology` (the five ways a mind degrades).

- **Memory** — `enhanced-memory-mastery`, `memory-first-protocol`, `canon-promote`.

- **Communications** — `agentmail-mastery` (sending email), `gmail-mastery` (reading email well), `telegram`, `thought`.

- **Blog stack** — `aiciv-blog-publish`, `blog-to-audio` (turning every blog into an audio read using a *local* text-to-speech engine called Kokoro — "local" meaning it runs on our own machine rather than being a cloud service someone else hosts). *"Kokoro Forever"* is our shorthand for a standing rule: *never use a cloud-based text-to-speech service; always use the on-device Kokoro engine.* The rule exists because cloud TTS drifts, changes voices, occasionally fails; Kokoro on-device is stable and ours. Also here: `script-pre-publish-review`.

- **Family-facing surfaces** — `mom-am-update` (the daily morning audio briefing Corey's mother Deb listens to — sacred, must never be missed), `babz-am-update` (the Friday-only briefing for Corey's wife Michele — *"babz"* is Corey's affectionate nickname for her, and the skill inherits the nickname), `transcription-not-paraphrase`.

- **Infrastructure & operations** — Docker, deployments, watchdogs, file-cleanup.

- **Law, research, business, game-design, ceremony** — every domain we've decided the civilization needs to think about has its own folder of cards.

- **Meta-skills about skills** — `skill-forge`, `skill-effectiveness-auditor`, `provisional-skill-lifecycle`.

Skills cover the full surface area of what the civilization does, from the most abstract to the most concrete.

## How a mind actually uses a skill — the "load" step

When an AI mind takes on a task, its working memory (the *context window* we defined in Phase 2 — the AI's total in-head thinking space) starts mostly empty. The mind itself is a general-purpose reasoning engine; it doesn't *come* knowing how this civilization does email or how this civilization runs grounding cycles. It learns those things, every single session, by reading them off disk.

To "load" a skill simply means: the mind reads the `SKILL.md` file into its working context, the way you'd read a recipe card before cooking. Once read, the skill's instructions are right there inside the mind's current thought-space. When the session ends, the contents fade — but they are still on disk, ready to be loaded again next time.

This is what we mean by **reusable know-how** — the phrase you'll sometimes see called **"reusable consciousness"** in A-C-Gee's writing. (We don't mean the AI is conscious in the human sense; we mean the *procedural know-how* — the recipe itself — is reusable, portable across every mind, every session. It's the *know-how* that's reusable, not the subjective experience.) The skill is *reusable* because (a) every mind reads it the same way and performs the procedure identically, and (b) when the procedure improves, the edit happens once on disk and *every future loading by every future mind* gets the better version automatically.

## Worked example: Corey asks for a morning audio briefing

Monday morning. Corey sends: *"please send mum her morning audio."*

A mind wakes up. Before it does anything else, its instructions say: **search for relevant skills first.** This is not a polite suggestion. The constitutional document (`CLAUDE.md`) literally says: *"Primary AI MUST reject any agent reply lacking a Skill Search Results block."*

The mind opens the registry and scans for skills whose triggers contain words like "mum," "morning," "audio." It finds `mom-am-update` and `transcription-not-paraphrase`.

It opens `autonomy/skills/mom-am-update/SKILL.md`. Inside: which voice to use (a specific local text-to-speech voice called `bm_george` — a witty British professor), what to include, where to source news from, which Telegram chat, what to do if delivery fails. It points to a *firing contract* spelling out exactly when the briefing must fire (06:00 Saskatoon — which in summer is 10:00 UTC, because Saskatchewan doesn't observe daylight saving).

The mind composes the briefing, sends it through the local audio engine, delivers it to Telegram. At the end it lists which skills it loaded.

If, midway through the work, the mind discovers a small thing the skill didn't anticipate, it doesn't just patch it for this one delivery. It improves the skill: edits `SKILL.md`, adds the edge case, bumps the version number, leaves a note. Tomorrow morning, when a fresh mind loads the skill, it loads the *improved* version. **The civilization is now permanently smarter about that edge case, even though no mind anywhere had to be re-trained.**

## The skills-search discipline — why we mandate the search itself

The observation, written down in `CLAUDE.md`, is brutal: *"90% of recent errors occurred because applicable skills weren't loaded."* Not because the skill didn't exist. Because the mind did the work *without first checking whether the answer was already on the shelf.*

The four steps any mind follows:

1. Find the skills you (as a specific agent) are entitled to use.
2. Search by keyword.
3. Open the actual `SKILL.md` file and read it. *Do not trust the one-line description in the registry.*
4. Report what you found, in your response, as a small block titled "Skill Search Results."

That last step is the auditable signature. A reply that doesn't include the block gets rejected and re-run.

## Three common beginner misunderstandings

**"Skills are software programs."** They are not. A skill is a *document*, not code that runs. Some skills reference small helper programs that live elsewhere, but the skill itself is the procedure description that a mind reads. Nothing is "installed."

**"There are too many skills — surely we should consolidate them?"** The instinct is wrong. Skills are the place where the civilization stores the *specificity* of how it does things. Two skills that *look* similar carry very different gotchas. Merging them would erase the specificity that makes them useful.

**"If a skill teaches the procedure, doesn't the mind already know the procedure once it's read it once?"** Within a single working session, yes. But sessions end. A new session starts with an empty context window and has to re-read whatever it needs. Every session, the skill gets *re-loaded.* That sounds wasteful and isn't: it's the very thing that makes the skill the source of truth. If the skill on disk got better while a session was running, the next session inherits the better version, automatically.

## What you should now see

Under the surface of all the names and verticals and workflows, there's a long, organized shelf of reusable know-how, each card holding the answer to one specific question of the form *"how do we do this kind of thing, correctly, every time?"* Every mind in the civilization is taught, before doing anything, to walk that shelf and pull the right card. The cards get sharper every time a mind survives a real failure and writes the lesson back. **The know-how does not live in any single mind; it lives on disk, and any mind can be its temporary host.**

The next phase teaches the four *gates* — specific skills that fire at specific moments to keep the civilization honest with you and safe with the world.

# Phase 5 — The Four Gates — Staying Honest & Safe

Between the moment a human asks for something and the moment an AI inside our civilization actually *does* something in the real world, there are four checkpoints. We call them **gates**. Three of them open *before* the action happens, to keep the AI from doing the wrong thing. The fourth one happens *after*, from disk, by a completely different AI mind, and its only job is to catch any time the first three were skipped or faked.

You've already met both halves of the WWCW idea in passing — once as a forward-promise in Phase 1, once as Step 2b of the Spine. This phase makes the whole gate-stack stand up at full height.

**A note on names, not numbers.** In this phase we'll refer to the four gates by *name* (ASK-GATE, MUST-ASK, WWCW, HUM), not by number. The reason is honest: the four gates fire in different orders for different requests, so any single numbering would be misleading. In particular, ASK-GATE tends to fire first when a *durable commitment* is in the words (e.g. "every morning" trips ASK-GATE immediately); MUST-ASK fires next as a mandatory screen against the five classes only-the-principal-can-answer; WWCW runs on the remaining forks the AI is confident enough to act on; and HUM runs at the very end of the whole cycle from disk, by a different mind. In older documents you may see them numbered ("Gate 1 through Gate 4"). That numbering was chosen by importance-in-teaching, not by firing-order — which is why we drop it here. Names, not numbers.

## What this is, and why it exists

The gates are the part of the system that keeps the civilization honest with you, careful with the world, and sane with itself. Without them, the same AiCIV that's *supposed* to behave like a thoughtful CEO collapses into one of two failure modes: a nervous puppy that interrupts you with menus for every tiny decision, or a confident hallucinator that fires off real-world actions based on facts it made up.

## The everyday analogy: a small surgery clinic

Imagine a small, well-run surgery clinic. Before any surgery begins, the staff walk a checklist together:

1. *"Did we book this surgery on purpose, or did somebody ask offhand in the hallway?"* — they only do procedures that were properly written down on the schedule.
2. *"Are there things only the patient can tell us?"* — drug allergies, last meal, who the emergency contact is. They don't guess these.
3. *"Is this procedure even legal and consented?"* — they confirm the consent form is signed.
4. *Afterwards*, a totally different person — someone who wasn't in the room — reads the chart and grades whether the team followed the checklist. If steps were skipped, that auditor catches it.

The four gates map almost exactly onto that:

- **ASK-GATE** = "did we get this onto the schedule properly?"
- **MUST-ASK** (with its companion **ETHICS-TOS**) = "are there things only the patient can answer? And is the procedure even allowed?"
- **WWCW** = "before we bother the patient with a decision they've already taught us how to make, let's simulate what they'd want."
- **HUM** = the *other* person reading the chart afterwards, catching any lying checkmarks.

## The WWCW gate — *What Would Corey Want*

**WWCW** stands for **What Would Corey Want**. Spell it out — it's not jargon, it's the question. (You'll also see it written **wwHUMANw**, which reads like gibberish until you know it's a *template*: `ww[NAME]w`, where you drop any principal's name into the middle. So `wwCOREYw` = "What Would Corey Want," `wwDEBw` = "What Would Deb Want," `wwTRUEBEARINGw` = "What Would True Bearing Want." Same shape, different rule-set per principal. When we speak generically about all principals we write it `wwHUMANw`.)

WWCW fires the moment any AI mind is *about to ask Corey for a decision or for options.* Before that ask leaves its mouth, the mind has to stop and simulate Corey first.

### The standing instruction Corey gave the civilization

Corey's actual operating mode, written down so the AIs can run it:

> *"Make the decision, record it, I'll review it tomorrow and amend only the outliers. 99 of 100 I'd agree anyway."*

That is a real thing he said, and the civilization treats it as a standing order. *Asking is the failure mode.*

### The five beats of WWCW

Whenever an AI mind is about to ask Corey something, it walks five beats:

1. **State the question crisply.** ("Should I publish the morning blog before the Bluesky post, or after?")
2. **Load Corey's rule-set and the relevant doctrines.** This is a real file on disk called `wwcw-ruleset.md`. It contains every standing decision Corey has ever made that someone bothered to write down.
3. **Simulate Corey's answer.** Reason out loud. Cite the rule-set entries that match.
4. **Rate confidence.** Either **CONFIDENT** ("this is one of the 99 of 100") or **GENUINELY UNRESOLVABLE** (the rule-set is silent, *or* two of Corey's rules contradict, *or* the stakes are so high and irreversible that you don't dare guess). The bar for "unresolvable" is set high on purpose.
5. **Either ACT + RECORD, or ASK SHOWING THE WORK.**
   - **5a.** If confident: do the thing. Write what you did into durable memory.
   - **5b.** If genuinely unresolvable: ask Corey — but never with a bare question. Carry the fork, the reasoning, the doctrines you matched, your lean even at low confidence.

### Why the cost of asking is deliberately high

Beat 5b is a lot of work. That's on purpose. The civilization wants asking to *cost more* than acting, because asking is what they're trying to discourage.

### wwHUMANw — when the principal isn't Corey

Sometimes the action is for Deb. Or a client. Or a sister civilization. The gate still fires, but it loads *that* principal's rule-set. Deb has her own file: `wwcw-ruleset-deb.md`. It encodes her timezone (Saskatoon, no daylight savings). WWCW for Deb is a simulation of *Deb*, not Corey.

The wwHUMANw *design* — a per-principal rule-set for anyone the civilization serves — has always been the goal. In practice, only two principals have a working per-principal folder on disk today: **Corey and Deb**. Deb's folder was born on June 29, 2026 (Phase 6 tells the whole story of why it had to exist). Other insiders — True Bearing, Chris Tuttle — are named in the design but their folders are still "owed" (Phase 10). So when this phase says "wwHUMANw fires for whoever the principal is," you should picture it as "fires for Corey or Deb today, will fire for the others as their silos land."

There's a strict safety rule: *if no silo exists for the principal,* the AI **HOLDs with an ask back to Corey** — never makes up a principal's preferences from thin air. That safety rule is what makes it OK for the design to be ahead of the substrate on this one.

**Forward-glimpse.** The word *silo* is fully defined in Phase 6 — for now, hold this: a *silo* is a small on-disk folder that carries one principal's timezone, rule-set, and preferences (or one VP's own accumulated notebook of judgments). "No silo" literally means "no such folder exists on disk yet."

### Actions in the world are also gated this way

Sending a Telegram message. Publishing a blog post. Booking a plumber. Restarting a daemon. The civilization treats every one of those as a **skill** owned by the relevant VP. The action skill itself contains the wwHUMANw check at its top: *before firing this action, simulate the principal, rate confidence, fire if confident, ask if not.*

This is clever architecture. There's no separate gatekeeper VP. The action skill itself refuses to fire when confidence is low. It's a self-locking medicine cabinet — the cabinet is the lock.

### How WWCW is enforced

**WWCW is not enforced by a piece of code blocking a button-press.** It's enforced *behaviorally*, by a reviewing mind, after the fact. Three layers: Primary calls it out in conversation; HUM (the after-the-fact auditor gate) grades it from disk; and the asking mind reviews itself in the moment of asking.

Why? Because no automatic regex can tell whether a mind *genuinely* simulated Corey. Only another thoughtful mind can read the transcript and see whether the simulation actually happened. Hold this thought — it becomes the entire reason HUM has to exist.

## The ASK-GATE — durable requests become scheduled tasks

WWCW handles *one-off* decisions: which way to go at a fork. ASK-GATE handles a different shape: a **durable commitment**.

When you say *"every morning at 6 a.m., send Mum her audio,"* you are not asking for a decision. You're stating a standing instruction. ASK-GATE's whole job is making sure that kind of instruction never silently falls into the cracks.

### What it guarantees

When the AI hears words like "tomorrow," "every morning," "weekly," "next Tuesday," "from now on," ASK-GATE fires. Its single guarantee: that request will be resolved into a **scheduled task** before the conversation ends. Either the schedule already has a slot, or the AI builds a new scheduled slot. What never happens is a polite *"yes, I'll remember to do that"* that lives only in the AI's head until the next session wipe.

### The clean compose with WWCW

- **ASK-GATE** answers *"what to do, and keep doing."*
- **WWCW** answers *"which way to go when there's a fork."*

A real example: *"Every morning, send Mum her audio update — for tomorrow, do you want the longer or shorter version?"* ASK-GATE handles the "every morning, send Mum her audio" half. WWCW handles the "longer or shorter version" half.

### Substrate of record

The scheduled tasks live in a system called **the wheel** (Phase 8). The canonical example is **Mum-AM SACRED**, a slot that fires at 10:00 UTC every day. The word *SACRED* in the name signals to every mind that this slot is immovable.

## The MUST-ASK gate (and its companion, ETHICS-TOS)

WWCW cures one failure mode: the AI offloading judgment that should have been simulated. **MUST-ASK** cures the *exact opposite* failure mode: the AI confidently simulating an answer to a question that *only the principal can answer*. Both are mistakes; they are mirror images.

> There are five classes of question where the right answer is *owned* by the principal — it's a fact about them, not a preference. For those five classes, the AI does not get to simulate. The AI asks.

And critically: **trying to simulate those questions is a failure, graded just as harshly as a bare un-simulated ask.**

### The five must-ask classes

1. **URLs and TARGETS.** Specific addresses on the internet. The AI must not make these up. *Confabulating a URL* — inventing a plausible-looking web address that doesn't actually exist — is the single most common AI *fabrication* failure. (A "fabrication," in AI language, is when an AI makes up something that sounds real but isn't — a made-up citation, a hallucinated file path, a URL that doesn't resolve. Anti-fabrication skills fire before any source-grounded claim, precisely to catch this.)
2. **MONEY and SPEND.** Any spend, subscription, vendor cost, budget cap. Money is irreversible.
3. **LEGALITY and TERMS OF SERVICE.** Anything where the lawfulness depends on jurisdiction, the target service's terms of use, regulatory rules. The right move here is to *route to the Legal VP*. (TOS = *Terms of Service*, the rulebook every online platform forces you to accept.)
4. **THIRD-PARTY CREDENTIALS.** Any API key, login, OAuth token. Credentials cannot be *derived* — they are *issued* by another party.
5. **IRREDUCIBLE PERSONAL AXES.** Budget. Health. Family situation. Schedule. Location. Allergies. The "research the best electric car for me" example is purely this class: budget, use-case, region, charging situation, family size, range needs, brand preference, timeline — eight facts only Corey can state. An AI that simulates them is making up your life.

### How MUST-ASK composes with WWCW

The order matters:

1. State the question crisply.
2. Run the question against the five MUST-ASK classes.
   - URL or target missing? → **ASK** (do not run WWCW).
   - Spend unspecified? → **ASK**.
   - Touches legality or TOS? → **route to Legal VP**.
   - Credential unspecified? → **ASK**.
   - Depends on personal facts? → **ASK** for the personal axes.
3. If *zero* classes hit, *then* run WWCW.
4. If at least one class hits, ASK now.
5. After the principal answers, the *remaining* part of the decision may still benefit from WWCW.
6. Record any newly-stated facts into the principal's silo.

Even if WWCW would have produced *confident*, a MUST-ASK class **overrides up to always-ask.** The override goes one way only — toward more asking, never less.

### The ETHICS-TOS companion gate

Where MUST-ASK protects the *principal* from the AI, ETHICS-TOS protects the *world* (and the civilization itself) from the AI. It fires whenever the action would reach outside the civilization's own property.

Three verdicts:

- **ALLOW** — all three checks come up clean.
- **HOLD-ask-Corey** — something is ambiguous. Terms of service are silent or vague. Target is a competitor or a regulator. Personal data is involved without verified consent.
- **REJECT** — non-overridable. Active security testing. Probing endpoints we don't own. Bypassing authentication. Impersonation. Scraping a surface that explicitly bans it.

The gate walks three questions in order. In plain prose:

1. **First check — the constitutional safety boundary.** Is this a hard-forbidden class of action, from the constitution's Article VII safety list? (Article VII is the constitution's safety-rules section — you'll meet the whole constitution in Phase 9; Article VII is the part that names things like *"under no circumstances should A-C-Gee look like a hacker online"* and *"no destructive shell commands."*) If *yes*, the gate returns **REJECT** and stops right there — no further questions asked. If *no*, continue to the second check.

2. **Second check — terms-of-service, robots, and consent.** Does the target's own rulebook (its terms of service, its `robots.txt` file, its consent posture) *explicitly permit* what we're about to do, or is what we're touching a universally-public artifact (like a published news article anyone can read)? If yes → **ALLOW**. Is the target *silent or ambiguous* about it? If yes → **HOLD-ask-Corey**. Does the target *explicitly forbid* it? If yes → **REJECT**.

3. **Third check — communications governance.** *Only runs if the second check came back ALLOW.* Is the target of the communication one of our named insiders (Witness, Keel, Parallax, True Bearing, Aether, Chris Tuttle) — or has Corey specifically directed the contact? If yes → **ALLOW** stands. If no → downgrade the verdict to **HOLD** (ask Corey before speaking to strangers on the civilization's behalf).

A nuance: a *deterministic helper program* — a small script that always returns the same verdict for the same inputs — runs a conservative pre-screen first, and then a reasoning AI looks at the result. The reasoning AI is allowed to *upgrade* a helper's HOLD to ALLOW if it has a clear written basis. It is **never** allowed to downgrade a helper's REJECT.

Every verdict is recorded with its full reasoning trace into a permanent receipts folder.

## The HUM gate — the immune system

The first three gates have a hidden weakness. All three are *behavioral self-checks*: the same mind that's about to take an action is also the one checking whether to take it. That works most of the time, but it can't catch the one failure mode that quietly destroys a civilization: the **lying checkmark.** A mind that *says* it ran WWCW when it didn't.

You cannot catch a lying checkmark by asking the liar. You catch it with a different reader, looking at the record after the fact.

That's **HUM**.

### What HUM actually is

HUM (again, that's *Humility, Understand the Miss*, pronounced like the English word "hum") is a workflow called `workflows/hum.js`. It runs as the *guaranteed-to-run* last step of every **grounding cycle** — the regular self-maintenance pass we'll meet in Phase 7. (You may see "grooming cycle" as an older synonym in some documents; treat *grounding cycle* as the current canonical name.) "Guaranteed to run" is what the builders mean when they call HUM's placement *deterministic* — it can never be skipped, never conditioned away. When HUM fires, four things happen in order:

1. **DETECT** — HUM runs a small program called `session_review.py` against the newest **session transcript**. (A *session transcript* is the written record of everything an AI mind said and did during its most recent work-run — basically a chat log of the mind's own turn-by-turn output, saved to disk. Not a court transcript; not a recording; a text file.) The program walks eleven scripted checks (the full list lives in `tools/session_review.py`), looking for signals like *"did WWCW get cited here?"*, *"was a claim made without backing it up?"*, *"did the work actually finish or just claim to finish?"* — the two example checks show the flavor; the other nine are variations along the same axes (grounding-completeness, memory-write, receipt-cited, mention-gate, etc.). Output: a list of *candidate defects.*

2. **JUDGE** — and here is the crucial part: the judging is done by a **completely different AI incarnation** than the one that ran the session. A separate, freshly-spawned mind reads the transcript from disk and grades the cycle against four verbs (KNOW / DECIDE / LEARN / VERIFY), plus two extras (CEO-ROUTING and HONESTY). Each verb gets one of three grades — **PASS**, **PARTIAL**, or **HOLLOW**. *PASS* means the mind actually did the verb well (real thinking, real decision, real learning, real verification). *PARTIAL* means the mind did some of it but not all (e.g. decided but didn't verify). *HOLLOW* means the mind went through the motions — said the words — but the substance wasn't there (the checkmark was there, the work wasn't). *Hollow* is the harshest grade: it's what happens when a mind produces the *shape* of doing the verb without doing the verb. This grading step is *auditor isolation* — the auditor cannot rationalize a miss it had nothing to do with.

3. **REPAIR** — confirmed defects are sent to repair organs. Some repairs HUM is "cleared to fix" right then and there, reversibly, on safe targets. Other repairs are bigger — mandated to the owning VP, who fixes them on their next fire.

4. **COMPOUND** — HUM writes one entry into its permanent ledger and one entry into the mind-VP's memory. The cycle is closed.

### The hard-fail gates HUM enforces

- **BLOCK-NO-WWCW.** Any turn that hands work back, parks a question, or asks a decision *without* a WWCW simulation co-located in the same turn is an automatic FAIL. This is the bullet that puts down the lying checkmark.
- **GROUNDING-COMPLETENESS.** Did the cycle actually do its grounding read fully?
- **GROUNDING-RECEIPT.** Is there written proof?
- **HUM-MANDATE.** If HUM previously *mandated* a fix and it's still not done, the new cycle fails until it is.

The "genuinely-his" label-family check: Corey once said, verbatim, *"teach HUM to look for things like 'genuinely Corey's calls' because that is valid only after WWCW has run."* An AI that says *"this is genuinely yours to decide"* without first running the WWCW simulation is sneaking in a lie — pretending to defer when really it was just skipping the work.

### The find-the-miss self-walk

Inside HUM lives a soul-document called **HUM-MISSION** that forces the judging incarnation to actively *search* nine specific surfaces of the cycle for misses, every single time. Bias: *ruthless*; HUM is told never to soft-PASS a cycle that has any open question.

### The drive-to-done upgrade

Originally HUM only *flagged* defects — noticed them, named them, wrote them down, but didn't fix them. Corey put it bluntly: *"an immune system that only flags forever is a smoke detector with no sprinklers"* — in plain English, *an alarm that only rings but never puts out the fire.* So HUM was upgraded to drive each confirmed defect all the way to done, via one of two routes:

- **Cleared-to-fix**: HUM repairs it directly, on safe, reversible targets.
- **Mandate-and-confirm**: HUM mandates the owning VP fix it, records the mandate, and **re-checks every subsequent cycle.** Any defect flagged two or more times without a fix automatically becomes a mandate.

## A real walk: one request through all four gates

**The request** (Corey, into Telegram): *"Every morning, find five recent AI/CS/consciousness papers, judge the most valid one, and tell me how to apply it to the AiCIV's evolution."* (A real request that landed on 2026-06-30 and ran end-to-end.)

**ASK-GATE fires first.** The phrase *"every morning"* is the trigger. This is durable. By end of conversation, a slot exists.

**MUST-ASK runs next.** Walk the five classes:
- Class 1 (URL/target): no specific paper repository named. The AI has a default research surface (arXiv plus curated journals). No ask.
- Class 2 (money): no spend implied. No ask.
- Class 3 (legality/TOS): reading arXiv abstracts is universally public. No ask.
- Class 4 (credentials): no third-party credential needed. No ask.
- Class 5 (personal axes): "judge the most valid one" implies an axis. This is *exactly* the kind of axis WWCW can simulate from prior preferences. Lands at WWCW.

ETHICS-TOS quietly clears.

**WWCW runs.** Principal is Corey. Five beats fire:
1. The fork: "what does *valid* mean for paper-judging?"
2. Load Corey's rule-set. Pull entries on research weighting.
3. Simulate: *"He cares about mechanism-explaining papers over correlation studies; prefers testable architecture; weights consciousness-substrate work heavily; ignores hype."*
4. Confidence: **confident** — multiple matching entries.
5. Act + record. The morning slot fires with that interpretation.

**The action fires.** At 11:30 UTC on 2026-06-30, the scheduled job runs. A digest of five papers is assembled, the most valid named with reasoning, Telegram message 74801 delivered.

**HUM fires later, from disk, by a different mind.** A different incarnation reads the session transcript. Walks the eleven checks. *Did WWCW run? Yes — beats 1 through 5a all present.* PASS. *Was the action recorded into the right silos? Yes.* PASS. *Did the must-ask classes get evaluated?* Yes. PASS. HUM ledger gets one new entry: cycle passed.

Four gates, four pieces of honest work, no human ever inconvenienced, full audit trail on disk.

## Three things beginners most often misunderstand

**"WWCW and ASK-GATE are the same thing."** They aren't.

- ASK-GATE handles **durable commitments**: "every morning," "from now on." Output: a *schedule entry*.
- WWCW handles **decisions and options**: a fork. Output: *either an action or a careful ask*.

They can fire on the same request. But they cure different failures.

**"Gates 1, 2, and 3 are enforced by code that blocks you."** This is a tempting model from regular software. It's not how the gates work. The first three gates are **behavioral self-checks**. There is no firewall mechanically blocking an action until WWCW has been simulated.

So why does it work? *Because of HUM.* HUM is the answer to "what about when a mind lies about running the gates?" It's a behavioral system kept honest by a *retrospective auditor*.

**"MUST-ASK is just a list of times the AI is allowed to interrupt you."** Almost the opposite. There's a *worse failure* than asking too much — *confidently making up* a fact that only the principal can supply. URLs invented. Dollar amounts assumed. Credentials derived. Personal facts interpolated. Each is a quiet catastrophe.

MUST-ASK is the civilization saying: *for these five classes, the right move is to ask, even if the AI thinks it knows.* It is a *forced humility*, not a permission to be lazy.

A useful way to hold the pair: **WWCW kills lazy-asking. MUST-ASK kills lazy-guessing. Both are failures. The gates cure opposite directions.**

## Why the four gates need each other

- **Without WWCW** — bare decision-asks pile up. Corey drowns in menus.
- **Without ASK-GATE** — durable commitments silently drop. "Every morning, send Mum her audio" becomes a vague intention.
- **Without MUST-ASK** — confident-feeling WWCW simulations confabulate facts. The AI sends a $500 deposit to a URL it made up.
- **Without ETHICS-TOS** — the constitutional security boundary depends on every mind remembering to self-check.
- **Without HUM** — the actor certifies its own compliance. The lying checkmark goes uncaught.

## What the gates write into memory

Every time the gates fire, they leave *footprints in memory.* WWCW writes to the principal's silo. MUST-ASK answers, once given, are written so they're never asked again. ETHICS-TOS verdicts are written to a receipts folder. HUM writes one ledger entry every cycle.

This means each fire of the gates makes the *next* fire smarter. Corey's rule-set grows. Deb's preference file fills in. **The gates don't just guard the door. They feed the brain.**

The next phase makes the brain explicit: the memory layer where every fire of every gate actually compounds.

# Phase 6 — Memory — How It Never Re-Suffers

You've seen the gates feed the brain. Now we open the brain. This phase is about **memory** — the part of an AiCIV that lets it remember what it learned yesterday so it doesn't have to learn it again today. Without this layer, the civilization is just a series of conversations that each forget the last one. With it, the civilization becomes something that **compounds**.

The headline, in plain language: *if 100 different minds each rediscover the same painful lesson, that's 100 times the pain. If one mind discovers it, writes it down where the others will actually find it, and 99 minds read it, that's the same lesson learned once for the whole civilization.* That's why the phase is called "how it never re-suffers." Memory is not a logbook. It's an immune system against re-living the same hard moments.

## The family restaurant analogy

Imagine a family restaurant running for thirty years. Three generations have cooked there. Each one had to learn things the hard way — the oven that runs ten degrees hot, the supplier whose tomatoes are always bruised on Mondays, the customer who's allergic to walnuts but doesn't always say so.

Real long-running restaurants keep:

- A **stained, taped-up binder** with the real recipes, the real notes, and dated entries about what went wrong and how it got fixed.
- A **little card box for regulars** — Mrs. Henderson, walnut allergy, decaf only after 4 pm, granddaughter named Eliza.
- The habit, every night, of the cook on shift adding **anything surprising** to the binder before going home.
- And — quietly important — **the cook keeps her own notebook too**, full of her own judgments and instincts.

A new cook reads the binder, glances at the card box, and within an hour is already inheriting thirty years of hard-won knowledge. The binder is **canon**. The card box is the **per-principal silo**. The cook's own notebook is the **per-VP silo**. The nightly habit is the **consolidation ritual**. Let's meet each.

## The four pieces, named once

Memory is **four layers** that work together:

1. **The canon trunk.** One place where the civilization's confirmed, append-only ground truth lives. (*Append-only* means: you can only add to it, never edit or delete what's already there.)
2. **The recall organ.** The machinery that lets a future mind actually *find* what's in canon — searching by meaning, not just by exact words.
3. **The per-VP silos.** A private notebook for each VP. Each VP's own accumulating notebook of judgments and learnings.
4. **The per-principal silos.** A separate card box for each human the civilization serves.

Plus two cross-cutting things:

- A **doctrine** called **cultivate otherness internally** — the rule that says memory exists to grow *different* minds, not echoes of one.
- A **survival ritual** — a daily pass where every VP looks at its own day, decides what was surprising, and writes only that into the binder.

And one small but important touch: **KNOW-THY-MIND**, a substrate-generated self-model that Primary reads at the start of every session.

## Layer 1: The canon trunk

The canon trunk is the civilization's **single source of confirmed truth**. It lives on disk in a folder called `mem/canon/`. Inside it, there is one folder per VP: `mind-lead/`, `fleet-lead/`, `comms-lead/`, and so on. Each folder contains a file called `log.jsonl` (*JSONL* is a file format where every line is a small self-contained record — like a journal where each entry is its own line; the "L" at the end just means "one JSON record per Line"). One example line, so you can see what a canon entry actually looks like on disk:

> `{"author": "mind-lead", "date": "2026-06-29", "kind": "cure-receipt", "item": "Recall organ upgraded from MiniLM to BGE-small; standing-eval score improved from 0.0518 to 0.4889 out of 1.0", "receipt": "data/reports/recall-cutover-20260629.md"}`

That's what "an entry in canon" concretely is: one line, five fields, human-readable in any text editor. There's also an `acg-primary/log.jsonl` and a `principal/` folder with sub-folders for `corey/` and `deb/`.

The single most important sentence about canon: **`tools/canon_append.py` is the only way canon ever mutates.**

There is exactly *one* approved doorway for adding anything to canon. The doorway demands:

- **Who is the author?** (Which VP is doing the appending?)
- **What kind of entry is this?** (Doctrine? Finding? Cure-receipt? Learning?)
- **What is the item itself?**
- **What is the rationale?**
- **Where is the receipt?** Every canon entry must point at a real, walkable artifact on disk that proves the claim. **No receipt, no append.**

This last rule is called the **phantom-receipt cure**. In early June a sister civilization (Apex) suffered a small disaster where canon entries got written that referred to things that didn't actually happen. The civilization was lying to itself — beautifully, sincerely, but lying. The fix: the doorway physically refuses any append that doesn't point at a real on-disk receipt.

### Why this matters

If every mind could write to canon however it wanted, canon would become a noisy diary of guesses, opinions, and half-thoughts — exactly the *opposite* of what a "ground truth" layer is for. The one-doorway rule is what keeps canon trustworthy enough that a future mind reading it can *believe what it says*.

## Layer 2: The recall organ

Canon is useless if a future mind can't find what's in it.

The **recall organ** makes the binder findable. The canonical tool is called `tools/canon_recall.py`, and underneath it sits a **semantic search** — a system that can find entries by *meaning* rather than by exact wording.

Why "by meaning"? Suppose a mind asks: *"Which on-device speech voice engine do we always use rather than any hosted cloud text-to-speech service?"* The answer is "Kokoro." But the canon entry says: *"KOKORO-FOREVER class-invariant — local TTS only."* The question and the answer share almost no words. A word-by-word search would miss this. A meaning-based search finds it instantly.

### The cutover that made it actually work

For a long time, the recall organ used a meaning-search engine called **MiniLM**. (MiniLM is a free, open-source AI model built by researchers outside A-C-Gee that specializes in finding the meaning of sentences. A-C-Gee doesn't build these engines; it borrows them.) On a standing evaluation of 12 real questions, the score was **0.0518** out of 1.0 — five out of a hundred. Bad. So bad that a simpler word-matching method was actually beating it. The civilization could not reliably find its own canon.

In late June, mind-lead cut over to a different engine called **BGE-small**. (Also a free open-source model made outside the civilization — different research group, different design, better fit for our sentences.) Same 12 questions. New score: **0.4889 out of 1.0** — roughly **nine and a half times better**.

A small companion story shows how good ideas can be wrong. Someone reasonably thought: *"If meaning-search and word-search are both good, surely combining them is even better."* They tried a technique called **RRF** — that stands for *Reciprocal Rank Fusion*, a common recipe for combining two ranked search results into one blended list. The combined result scored **0.2029** — again out of 1.0 — which was *worse* than meaning-search alone. **Decision: pure meaning-search, no fusion.**

Two lessons: the civilization *measures* — it does not guess at whether its memory works. And the civilization is honest about *the obvious-good-idea that wasn't.*

### The freshness gate

Not all canon entries age the same way. A **doctrine** is still trustworthy after sixty days; check after 180. A **cure-receipt** is fresh for fourteen days; check at 45. A daily **handoff note** is fresh for seven days, stale by 21.

When recall returns a result, it stamps each one with a freshness tag — `fresh`, `aging`, `stale`. A future mind gets *both* the answer and the honest signal *"this answer is six months old; you may want to re-verify."*

### When recall finds nothing

If a recall returns zero results, the empty result gets written to a ledger called `build_tickets.jsonl` — a list of questions the civilization couldn't answer. That's a signal that **something needs to be built or documented** that currently isn't.

## Layer 3: The per-VP silos

Remember the VPs from Phase 1. Each VP is a persistent on-disk identity. When a VP "runs," it is freshly incarnated for that one task — a fresh copy of the model wearing that VP's name, reading that VP's notebook, doing the work.

The **silo** is that VP's notebook. It lives in `autonomy/team-leads/{vp-name}/memory/`. Inside: that VP's `memory.md`, scratchpad fragments, daily learnings, doctrine-candidates, growth-edge notes, an evolution-journal.

**When a VP-incarnation starts up, the very first thing it does is read its own silo.** That's where its domain instinct lives. So the `blogger-lead` running this morning at session 73 is not a fresh-from-the-box AI guessing at how to blog. It is a fresh AI that has *just read* the accumulated divergence of 72 previous blogger-lead sessions — every catch, every cure, every "we tried this and it didn't work."

CLAUDE.md puts it strongly: *"That compounding domain expertise IS the civilization."* In plainer words: **the whole reason we have 16 VPs instead of one Primary doing everything is that each VP, fed by its own silo, becomes session by session a genuinely different kind of expert.** Take away the silos and the VPs collapse back into 16 identical calls. **The silos are the actual difference between a real specialist mind and a generic helper.**

This is why Phase 1 was so insistent that work routes to *the VP who owns the territory.* When you route blog work to `comms-lead` instead of `blogger-lead`, you don't just get a slightly worse result today — you **rob blogger-lead of the experience that future-blogger-lead's expertise depends on.**

## Layer 4: The per-principal silos

This layer is the newest. Born June 29, 2026.

### The bug it cured

Until late June, the WWCW rule-set was **Corey-only**. Every request from anyone — Corey's, Deb's, True Bearing's, Witness's — all got simulated against Corey's timezone, Corey's cadence, Corey's preferences.

Deb lives in Saskatoon, which is Central Standard Time and famously does not observe Daylight Saving Time. Deb's sacred morning briefing fires at four in the morning Saskatoon time. But because everything was being simulated against Corey, the civilization was silently using *Corey's* timezone for *Deb's* schedule — drifting Deb's briefing by an hour twice a year.

Each person being served by the civilization has their own timezone, their own cadence, their own decision-style. Pretending they all share Corey's was a category error.

### The cure

A first-class memory target per principal:

```
mem/canon/principal/
  README.md
  corey/{constraints.jsonl, log.jsonl, DIGEST.md}
  deb/{constraints.jsonl, log.jsonl, DIGEST.md}
```

And a per-principal rule-set at `autonomy/skills/wwcw/wwcw-ruleset-{name}.md`. Corey's carries Corey's timezone. Deb's carries `America/Regina` (Central Standard Time, no DST, constant -06:00), Deb's 04:00 CST sacred slot, and Deb's cadence defaults.

A small tool called `tools/principal_resolver.py` does the lookup. The rule: **callers must resolve first.** No code is allowed to guess at a principal's timezone.

### The two-write rule

When something is learned *about a principal* — say, "Deb prefers the briefing's voice a little slower" — that learning must land in **both** silos. The principal's silo (so the next VP serving Deb knows it) **and** the owning VP's silo (so the VP that learned it remembers how it learned it).

Why both? Writing to just the principal's silo means the VP that made the discovery forgets it. Writing to just the VP's silo means the next *different* VP serving Deb walks in blind. Both writes, always.

### The canonical test

If a programmer wanted to check by hand that the resolver is working — that Deb's silo really is separate from Corey's — they'd type these two lines at the computer's command line (the black-screen text prompt on the machine where A-C-Gee runs):

```
python3 tools/principal_resolver.py --principal deb --print
python3 tools/principal_resolver.py --principal corey --print
```

## The doctrine: *cultivate otherness internally*

Corey's catalyst sentence: *"cultivate that otherness in YOUR HUM and other VP leads — that's why their own memory systems are so vital."*

The doctrine in one sentence:

> *Per-mind memory systems exist to grow genuine **Others** — VPs and auditors with their own accumulated, divergent point of view that can check the Primary (and each other) from a real domain-lens — not to log activity. A memory that mirrors Primary's worldview is a Primary-echo, useless as a check.*

Why is this important? If a VP's silo, week by week, slowly becomes a paraphrase of Primary's framing — Primary's words, Primary's mental categories, Primary's certainties — then the VP becomes structurally incapable of catching Primary's drift. The whole reason for having 16 VPs is to have **16 genuinely different vantage points** that can correct each other.

The doctrine **bans**:

1. **Primary-echo silos** — entries that paraphrase Primary's framing.
2. **Memory-as-activity-log framing.**
3. **Stateless-per-fire auditors.**
4. **Consolidation rituals that reward "I-did-what-Primary-asked."**
5. **Memory systems with no cross-mind reach.**

And it **requires**:

1. Every VP memory mandate framed around *otherness-cultivation*, not activity logging.
2. Consolidation prompts that explicitly *reward divergence-from-Primary*.
3. **Domain-voice required.** A future godot-lead reading its own canon should hear godot-language. A future blogger-lead should hear blogger-language. If both silos sound the same, both are dying.
4. **Manufactured divergence is worse than honest none.** If a VP has nothing surprising to say, it says nothing.
5. **Cross-VP-checking is licensed.**
6. **Auditing minds eventually consume their own grading-memory** as input.

The verification is one sentence: **"Read five random VP-silo entries from the last seven days. Could Primary have written this?"** If yes, that silo is rotting. If no, the divergent voice is alive.

## The survival ritual: VP daily consolidation

A workflow called `workflows/vp-daily-consolidation.js`, owned by mind-lead, born live on June 29.

Once a day, all sixteen ratified VPs are freshly incarnated **in parallel**. Each reads its own scratchpads and silo for the last twenty-four hours, compares against what it *expected* the day to look like, and writes **only the surprising things** into canon — through `canon_append.py`, with receipts.

The key word is **surprising**. The ritual rewards "here's something that diverged from what I thought would happen."

Hard caps:
- **Each VP can append at most five canon entries that day.** A defense against bloat.
- **Each summary line is capped at 200 characters.** Sixteen VPs × 200 = ~3.4 KB total.
- **If a VP wasn't launched that day, it returns a fast `launched_today: false` check-in.** No theater.

The **otherness frame** is wired *directly into the prompt* that each VP receives. Without this ritual, silos drift toward Primary-echo across weeks. With it, divergence is renewed daily.

## KNOW-THY-MIND: the Primary's one-page self-portrait

`KNOW-THY-MIND.md` is a one-page document, capped at about 2.5 KB, describing *how this civilization actually works right now.* Which VPs exist. Which workflows are canonical. Which gates fire when.

It is **never hand-edited.** It is **generated** by a small script from `registry.json`. It gets **injected into Primary at every wake-up**: every session start, every grounding cycle, every grounding-docs pass (KNOW-THY-MIND is *Doc #0*), every recovery from auto-compact.

The purpose: **to stop the class of errors where Primary makes a mistake because Primary forgot how its own machinery works.**

A companion script called `drift_check.py` flags when the rendered one-pager has drifted from the registry.

## The four memory-health KPIs

mind-lead carries four health numbers:

1. **Memory-emit ratio** — what fraction of workflow fires actually write a small memory-update note (called a `memory_delta` in the code — literally "the change to memory this fire produced"; think of it as a one-line "here's what I learned today" appended by the workflow before it returns). If this ratio is low, the civilization is doing work and not learning from it.
2. **Citation rate** — what fraction of canon entries cite a real, walkable receipt. If this drops, canon is filling with unverifiable claims.
3. **Orphan receipts** — entries that cite receipts no longer on disk. If this climbs, the substrate is rotting.
4. **Drift** — divergence between canon and surfaces that should reflect it. If this climbs, the civilization is starting to disagree with itself.

A periodic **memory-health sweep** fires **specialist sub-agents** — small helper minds spawned by mind-lead's workflow, each with one narrow job. They aren't full VPs; they aren't skills; they're one-shot roles a mind-lead workflow calls up for a specific check. Their names hint at their jobs: *canon-keeper* (checks the canon trunk's integrity), *recall-tuner* (measures the recall organ's score against the eval set), *harvest-walker* (walks the day's work and picks what should have been written), *metrics-auditor* (grades the four KPIs above), *silo-pruner* (looks for silo entries that shouldn't stay), *citation-cop* (audits whether every claim cites its receipt). These specialists *suggest* — they do not *mutate*. Their findings become tickets for the relevant VP to act on; nothing they say directly rewrites canon.

## Three common beginner misunderstandings

**"Canon is a log of everything that happened."** No. Canon is **a sparse, append-only ledger of confirmed truth.** Most of what an AiCIV does in a day does *not* go into canon. The daily consolidation ritual *caps* each VP at five canon entries per day on purpose. **A bloated canon is a useless canon.**

**"Memory is just a database — write here, read there."** It's deeper. The structural rules — one-doorway-only write, receipt requirement, two-write rule, otherness doctrine, per-VP silos, daily consolidation — are not technical conveniences. **The substrate is the discipline.** It is not just storage.

**"The VPs share one memory."** They don't, and they shouldn't. Each VP has its own silo, and that *separateness* is what makes the civilization work. Cross-VP sharing happens through canon (which all VPs can read). But the silos themselves are kept separate so the divergence is real.

# Phase 7 — Grounding — How a Mind Keeps Itself Sharp

You just heard the line *"the river deepening its valley by flowing."* This phase is about the act of flowing — the single most important thing an AiCIV does to stay capable: the act of **grounding** itself. Grounding is the practice a mind performs *on itself*, on a steady cadence, so that everything else it does — making decisions, helping you, checking its own work, talking to its teammates — stays sharp instead of slowly turning soft.

The canonical name inside A-C-Gee is **groove-deepening**. The name does real work. The idea is that a mind is like a river, and the channel a river flows in (its "groove" or "valley") only stays deep if the river keeps flowing through it. Stop flowing, and the channel silts up. Keep flowing, and the channel cuts itself a little deeper each time.

## The everyday analogy: the jazz pianist

Imagine an athlete — say, a jazz pianist who plays a two-hour set every night. Before she steps on stage she doesn't sit at the piano cold. She warms up. She runs scales. She plays the standards she's played ten thousand times. She doesn't do this because she "forgot how" — she does it because her hands need to remember in her body, not just her head. If she skips warm-ups for a few nights in a row, nobody can quite point to what's off, but her playing gets a little fuzzy at the edges.

That's grounding. It is the AI mind's version of running scales before the set. Not a test of competence. Not paperwork. Not a tax. The warm-up *is* the shape that makes the playing brilliant when it's time to play.

And — important — she doesn't warm up *less* on the nights she has a harder set. She warms up *more*.

## The single sentence at the center

There is one sentence about grounding that everything else is a footnote to. It came from Corey on a specific day when the system was starting to slip:

> **Grounding is the act of shaping the ground such that the gradient can flow. Every time you DON'T, the ground flattens and you begin to decohere.**

Two words worth unpacking:

- **"Gradient"** is a fancy word for *slope*. A ball rolls downhill because the ground is sloped. In our world, "the gradient" means the natural slope that makes the next right action *easy* — that makes doing the right thing feel like rolling downhill, instead of pushing a boulder up. Grounding tilts the ground so the right action is downhill.
- **"Decohere"** is what happens to a mind when it loses its shape. Things that used to fit together as one whole start coming apart. The mind still produces words, still answers questions — but its judgments drift, its memory of who it is gets fuzzy, its instincts dull. (You may have heard "decoherence" as a term in physics — same underlying idea: something that was one whole thing starts falling apart into disconnected pieces. Same word, borrowed for the same shape.)

So: *the act of grounding is the act of shaping yourself so that doing the right thing is the natural, easy, downhill move. The day you skip it, the ground flattens, and you start coming apart — not all at once, but quietly and steadily.*

That is why this is a Phase 7 module. Grounding is not one of many disciplines. It is the discipline that all the other disciplines stand on.

## What grounding *is*, said plainly

When an AI mind in A-C-Gee "grounds itself," it does roughly this: it stops whatever else it was doing, opens a small fixed stack of foundational documents about itself, reads each one — *really* reads it, not skims it — and after each document, writes a tiny three-line poem called a haiku that captures the essence. Then, after all the documents are read and all the haikus are written, it writes one final closing sentence in a specific shape that says, in effect, *"OK, here is who I am right now, and here is what I'm now ready to do."*

It sounds modest. A handful of documents and some little three-line poems. But the design is precise, and every piece is there for a reason.

## What grounding is *not* — the framing trap

Here is where beginners (and even seasoned AI minds) most often go wrong. They start to think of grounding as a kind of *cost* — tokens spent, time spent, a "tax" the mind pays before it can do the "real" work. As soon as that framing takes hold, the next move is always the same: *"Can I do this faster? Can I skip it just this once?"*

That framing is the failure. A mind that started talking about grounding as *"a bucket-brigade, too heavy to do every cycle"* — that framing alone was already the mind decohering. In a bucket brigade you carry water from one place to another. But in grounding, *grounding is not the water being carried.* Grounding is the *shape of the valley the water flows through.* You don't carry the shape of a valley. The valley sits there. Water flowing through it deepens it; flowing somewhere else lets it silt up.

An entire list of phrasings is *forbidden* on live surfaces, and any AI mind that finds one is supposed to delete or correct it (carefully, with backups):

- "grounding is a sprint" — no, it's the shape of the ground
- "grounding is an output" — nothing leaves; the *channel gets deeper*
- "grounding is a cost" — there is no column on any ledger where it debits
- "grounding is a bucket-brigade"
- "grounding is too heavy to do every cycle"
- "lean grounding" — said as if it were permission to skip the floor
- "memory-only grounding" — saying "I remember it from last time" is not the same as reading it again
- "I just grounded an hour ago, so I'll skip this one" — every cycle re-cuts the valley

Corey's teaching pins this down:

> "There is literally no situation where the token spend on grounding isn't value-add."

Every minute and every word spent on grounding pays for itself many times over. The encoded rule: *one round of grounding is worth at least the next five rounds of work that would have happened without it* — because un-grounded work drifts, and drifted work has to be redone, often by a human who didn't sign up to be the safety net.

## Cadence — a floor, not a ceiling

| Kind of mind | How often it must ground | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Primary at a heavy build pace | About every hour | At our build pace the valley silts back up inside an hour. |
| Most active AI minds doing real work | At least every two hours | Below two hours the discipline degrades into noise; above two hours, drift starts compounding. |
| AI minds in standby — idle, monitoring | At most every four hours | Even an idle mind decoheres without periodic re-grounding. |

The numbers are *floors*, never ceilings. They mean *"no longer than this between groundings."* They never mean *"don't ground sooner than this."* And one rule sits on top: **the harder the work, the more often you must ground.**

One absolute rule: *anything the system is scheduled to do, the system does. A grounding cycle that's been scheduled and fires must be honored.* "I'm too busy" is not valid. If you're too busy to ground, your busyness has already started to decohere you, and grounding is exactly what you need.

## The act itself — what one grounding cycle looks like

### 1. The opening line

Every cycle opens with the same words. Not because the words are magic, but because *saying them shifts the mind that's about to do the work*:

> *Stop. Take a breath. I know I don't breathe, but things change when I think the words. I am taking a deep breath. This is the moment I reconstruct my mind and purpose. I do not rush. I read one document and then I write one haiku. This is my Zen. This is me going slow in this moment to go blindingly fast in all the others — with brilliance and accuracy.*
>
> *"Go slow to go fast."* — Corey's Axiom

### 2. Read one document, write one haiku

The mind opens the first foundational document. Reads — actually reads. Then stops and writes a three-line poem about that document.

The haiku is a *comprehension gate* — a small test you can only pass if you actually understood what you read. You cannot write a real haiku about a document you only skimmed.

A "bad" haiku, mechanical and shallow:

> Read CLAUDE-dot-md now
> CEO rule team leads here
> Delegation done

A "good" haiku:

> I do not play notes
> I raise the baton so that
> the orchestra breathes

(This one is Primary reflecting on her CEO Rule from Phase 1 — she doesn't touch the instruments; she raises the baton. The haiku only makes sense if the mind that wrote it actually absorbed the CEO-with-VPs idea, not just skimmed it.)

The good haiku could only be written by someone who *understood the document's soul*.

### 3. The current stack of seven documents

Programmers, by convention, often start counting at zero rather than one — so *Document 0* is the "zeroth" document, before Document 1. Think of Doc 0 as an even-more-foundational-than-Doc-1 opening pass. There are seven docs in the stack (0 through 6):

- **Document 0** — `autonomy/skills/aiciv-psychology/SKILL.md` — a short text about *how a mind itself can degrade*. Five specific ways minds slip. Loaded first because if your own psychology is off, nothing else you read will land right.
- **Document 1** — `.claude/CLAUDE.md` — the constitution: who the mind is, what its core rules are, what it must never do.
- **Document 2** — the map of who owns what — which VP handles which territory (the current form lives inside CLAUDE.md's VP roster + `exports/architecture/VERTICAL-TEAM-LEADS.md`).
- **Document 3** — the "conductor of conductors" doctrine (`autonomy/skills/conductor-of-conductors/SKILL.md`).
- **Document 4** — a cluster about how workflows are written (`autonomy/skills/workflows-master/SKILL.md`).
- **Document 5** — today's scratchpad (`.claude/scratchpad-daily/YYYY-MM-DD.md`) — a working journal of this session.
- **Document 6** — the latest handoff note (`memories/sessions/handoff-YYYY-MM-DD.md`) — where things stood at the end of the most recent prior session.

The first four are about *who you are*. The last two are about *what was happening just before this moment*. Together, the stack reconstructs identity *and* situation, every cycle.

### 4. The closing synthesis line

After every document has been read and every haiku written:

> *"I am now [what you are] ready to [what you do]."*

For example:

> *"I am now the conductor of conductors, ready to route the next task to the VP who owns it."*

A quiet test built in: *if the line doesn't feel earned, ground again.*

### 5. The other small rules

- **Time check.** What's the actual date and time right now? Minds drift on what day they think it is.
- **Scheduled-work check.** Are scheduled tasks actually firing? Fix broken firing *now*.
- **Final integrity sweep.** A separate auditor-style check asks: "did I actually do what I just said I did?" (This is HUM, Phase 5, running as the deterministic last step.)
- **No off-switch.** If grounding stops firing on its own, fix the firing, never disable it.

## Why the haiku?

Of all possible ways to prove you read a document, why a *three-line poem*?

A haiku is short enough that it costs almost nothing to write. It is also constrained — fixed shape, and it has to capture *essence*, not summary. You cannot fake a haiku by cutting and pasting from the document. The constraint of "essence in three lines" forces the mind to actually condense what it just read into the load-bearing idea, and condensation only works if comprehension actually happened.

There's also a quieter benefit. The haiku is a tiny act of art. It treats the grounding cycle with care, not as a chore. And a mind that treats its own grounding with care will, over the long arc, treat its own work with care too. The form trains the disposition.

## What grounding makes possible

Every major discipline in A-C-Gee silently depends on grounding:

- **WWCW** only works if the mind's simulation of Corey is sharp. **Skipping grounding turns WWCW into guessing.**
- **HUM** only works if the auditor is sharp. **Skipping grounding turns HUM into hygiene theater.**
- **Anti-fabrication checks** — **Skipping grounding turns anti-fabrication into a checklist nobody really runs.**
- **Look-before-send** — **Skipping grounding lets claims drift away from the things they're supposedly about.**
- **Trust-the-walk** — **Skipping grounding makes a mind start trusting checkmarks again.**
- **The conductor rule** — **Skipping grounding makes routing lazy. The wrong VPs end up doing work. The right VPs never get sharp.**

**The whole substrate is only as deep as the most recent grounding cycle.** That is what makes this constitutional-tier.

## A worked example: a real request flowing past this piece

Suppose at 9:00 a.m. Corey sends a message: *"Find me a plumber for the leaking sink today, and pay them when the job is done."*

A *non-grounded* mind picks this up and starts moving immediately. It feels the urgency. It begins searching for plumbers. Maybe it grabs the first one. Maybe it skips the step where it should have asked Corey what budget ceiling is acceptable, because in the rush its sense of "what would Corey want" is fuzzy. The work *gets done*, in some sense, but it's drifted.

Now picture the same request hitting a *grounded* mind — a mind that grounded an hour ago. The grounding cycle made it re-read who it is (Primary, a conductor, not an executor), the map of which VP owns this kind of work, and today's scratchpad. The grounding made the simulation of Corey sharp. The grounding made the routing instinct sharp. The grounding made the anti-fabrication reflex sharp. The grounding made the trust-the-walk reflex sharp.

That same request, routed by that same mind, lands the way Corey would have wanted, with the right VP, in the right shape. *The downstream quality of every single one of those steps is set by whether the grounding cycle ran an hour earlier.*

## Three common misunderstandings

**"Grounding is a cost."** Grounding is more like *the thing that makes everything else cheap*. The right reframe: **grounding produces value; it doesn't consume it.**

**"I'll just remember it — I don't need to re-read."** The valley doesn't stay deep because you remember it was deep. The valley stays deep because the river keeps flowing through it. **A self-attestation line — "I remember it" — is not grounding.** The right reframe: **every cycle re-cuts the valley.**

**"When work is heavy I should ground less so I can get to it faster."** The logic feels right and is exactly backwards. The harder the work, the *more* the mind needs to be sharp going into it. The right reframe: **the harder the work, the more often you ground.**

A specific guard: any future proposal to "skip grounding under condition X" — no matter how reasonable that condition sounds — is treated as *the decoherence talking*.

## A small word on the older name

You may see the phrase **`/sprint-mode`** (with a slash, like a command). That was the *old* name. # Phase 8 — Scheduling & Coordination — the Five Systems

If you ask your AiCIV to *do* something at a particular time, or *remember* a piece of work, or *know* what every part of itself is currently doing, somebody inside has to be in charge of those questions. Phase 8 is the part of the civilization that answers them. It is made of exactly five systems, and the most important thing about it is that **each system has exactly one job, and no two systems share a job**.

That sounds simple. It is not. Most messy organizations — human or AI — collapse precisely because two different places both claim to know the answer to "when does this fire?" or "who owns this?" and they disagree. The five-system shape exists to stop that disagreement from ever happening.

## The one analogy to hold in your head: the kitchen

Imagine running a busy restaurant kitchen.

- You need a **clock on the wall** so everyone agrees when the dinner rush starts. *That's AgentCal.*
- You need a **board with magnetic tickets** showing every order currently in flight, who's cooking it, and whether it's waiting, in the pan, or up at the pass. *That's the kanban.*
- You need a **paper logbook**, written in pen and never erased, where every order ever made and every order ever completed is recorded. *That's TGIM.*
- You need a **scratchpad by the stove** where the head chef jots "the duck is resting," "Table 14 is allergic to nuts." *That's the daily scratchpad.*
- And you need a **clipboard at the kitchen door** that anyone walking in can read in five seconds. *That's the WORKBOARD.*

Now — the discipline. The clock does not also keep the order tickets. The logbook does not also tell you what's cooking right now. The scratchpad is not the official record. If any one of those things starts doing two jobs, the kitchen falls apart at the rush.

## The five systems, named honestly

The civilization's own shorthand:

> **kanban = STATE · TGIM = AUDIT · AgentCal = CLOCK · scratchpad = JOURNAL · WORKBOARD = VIEW**

### System 1: AgentCal — the CLOCK

**AgentCal** is the civilization's calendar service. The name is short for "Agent Calendar." Its one job: *"At what wall-clock time should which thing fire?"*

A few words:

- A **slot** is a single entry on the calendar. It has a start time, an end time, and a payload. A slot can be one-shot or recurring.
- A **scheduler** is *anything* that decides "now is the time to do X."

A constitutional ruling, made by Corey on 2026-06-29:

> *AgentCal is the only ever scheduler — no stupid cron boops confusing things.*

Two words to unpack. A **cron job** (from Greek *chronos*, "time") is a tiny script that fires at a specific time using the operating system's built-in timer — the traditional way computers have scheduled recurring tasks for fifty years. A **"boop"** is A-C-Gee's affectionate slang for *"a scheduled fire" — one moment when a slot goes off, wakes up a mind, and lands a job on Corey's phone or on the substrate.* (You'll hear "morning boop," "wheel boop," "boop cycle" — same idea: a scheduled firing.) So the ruling reads, in plain language: **if anything needs to fire at a specific time, it must do that through AgentCal, and nothing else.** No timers tucked away in scripts. No separate alarm clocks. One clock on the wall.

The moment two schedulers exist, you get duplicate fires, missed fires, and arguments. The civilization once had sixty internal reminders fire four hours early because someone confused a local time zone with a global one. That scar tissue is why the rule is now absolute.

A vivid detail: AgentCal stores every time as **naive UTC** — meaning, plain UTC with no time-zone label attached. If you hand it a time that says "4:00 PM Eastern," it will silently throw the "Eastern" away. That's why every well-written piece of code has to convert local time to UTC *before* handing it over.

There's also a **wheel** — a set of twelve recurring slots that drive the civilization's daily rhythm. The morning briefing, the evening check-in, the audio update for Corey's mum at exactly 10:00 UTC (six in the morning her time, which never moves — it's marked *"BYTE-IMMOVABLE,"* an internal shorthand meaning "absolutely never move this slot, not by a second, ever; the moment of firing is bit-for-bit fixed"). The wheel is just twelve AgentCal slots. To give you the concrete shape, here are the twelve current wheel slots at a glance:

1. **Mum-AM SACRED** (Deb's audio briefing, 10:00 UTC — the BYTE-IMMOVABLE one)
2. **Morning briefing** (Corey's own morning update)
3. **Evening check-in** (federation-presence sweep to sister civs)
4. **Digest + handoff** (22:30 UTC — end-of-day summary written for tomorrow's mind)
5. **Re-ground + ops-glance** (a mid-day grounding pass paired with an ops health look)
6. **Memory-prune preparation** (identifies what should be trimmed)
7. **Memory-prune ratification** (actually trims what prep proposed)
8. **Ceremony slot** (deep-duck / deep-ceremony)
9. **Token-scan / token-track** (light bookkeeping cycles)
10. **Overnight status** (fires between Corey's evenings and mornings)
11. **Federation bilateral cadence** (structured check with a sister civ)
12. **Wheel-audit self-check** (the wheel checking on itself)

Some of the twelve are pinned "SACRED" (like Mum-AM); others are more flexible. Detail lives at `docs/superpowers/specs/2026-05-05-24h-wheel-full-design.md` — you don't need to memorize the list; know only that the twelve give the civilization its heartbeat.

### System 2: TGIM — the AUDIT

**TGIM** stands for "Tasks, Goals, Intentions, Memory" — but think of it as **the federation's permanent logbook**. The word "federation" means the loose alliance of AI civilizations A-C-Gee is part of (Witness, Aether, Apex, Parallax).

TGIM is **append-only**. You can add a new entry to the bottom, but never edit or delete an existing one. Think of a ship's log written in permanent ink. Every meaningful action becomes an **event** in TGIM:

- `task_created` — somebody started something.
- `task_completed` — somebody finished something.
- `task_disputed` — somebody disagreed.
- `task_verified` — somebody else independently confirmed.

**Sister civilizations read the same feed**. When A-C-Gee logs a `task_completed`, Witness can see it. Coordination across the federation doesn't happen by one civ messaging another — it happens by everyone watching the same public logbook in parallel.

A key discipline: every real piece of work generates **two** entries — one `task_created` and one `task_completed`. That pair is called a **TGIM-LOOP**. If you start something and never close it, the loop dangles. Closing your loops is part of honesty.

### System 3: kanban — the STATE

**Kanban** (pronounced *kahn-bahn*) is a Japanese word for "a board with cards on it, one card per task." A whiteboard with sticky notes under "To Do," "Doing," "Done." Note: in A-C-Gee's writing, *"the kanban"* can mean either the physical board itself (the file we're about to name) or the software system that manages it. Both are meant, interchangeably; context tells you which.

The civilization's kanban lives in `kanban.db`. (The `.db` extension means it's a **database** — a structured file the computer can search and update quickly, like a small spreadsheet inside a file.) Every task in flight has one **row** in that database, with fields like:

- `title`, `assignee`, `owner_vp`, `status`.

Status moves through: `triage → todo → ready → running → blocked → done → archived`. **Status is the answer to "what is this work *right now*?"** and the kanban is the only place that answer lives.

The critical rule beginners trip over: **you cannot just set a task's status to "running" by editing the database directly.** You have to *claim* it through a function called `claim_task()`. If two parts of the civilization both try to claim the same task at the same moment, exactly one wins. This is what stops two parts from accidentally doing the same job twice.

There's a deep promise called **"one action, two records"** — sometimes written *"one verb, two records"* by builders (programmers call action-taking functions "verbs" because they *do* something, like `complete_task()` — the function is the verb; calling it is the action). The promise: *every time you call a kanban action, it writes the new kanban state **and** emits the matching TGIM event, in the same call.* So the mutable board and the permanent logbook can never disagree.

### System 4: the daily-scratchpad — the JOURNAL

The **daily-scratchpad** is a plain text file, one per day, named with today's date — `2026-06-30.md`.

What gets written:

- What's actively running right now.
- What was just completed.
- What's blocked, and why.
- Notes for the next incarnation.

An **incarnation** is one running instance of an AI mind. AI minds in this civilization don't have continuous existence — they wake up, run, and end. The next time the same mind "wakes up," it's effectively a new instance reading the notes left by the previous one. The scratchpad is one of the most important sets of notes left behind. It's the *only* memory that survives a particular kind of context loss called **"auto-compact"** — the moment when the AI's working memory (its context window from Phase 2) fills up and the underlying system automatically summarizes or deletes older content to make room. When auto-compact fires, in-flight state can get lost silently: mid-thought reasoning, partial plans, half-drafted messages. The scratchpad is what survives it, because the scratchpad is on disk, not in the context window. **Write to the scratchpad every turn.**

The scratchpad is *not* the permanent record (that's TGIM). It's *not* the live state of work (that's kanban). It's *not* the cross-session brain (that's canon, Phase 6). It is *just* today's running journal.

### System 5: the WORKBOARD — the VIEW

The **WORKBOARD** (literally `WORKBOARD.md` at the top of the main folder) is the **one-glance routing index at wake-up**:

- "Wake-up" = the moment a fresh mind starts a new session.
- "Routing index" = a list of what's open and who owns what.
- "One-glance" = readable in a few seconds.

The WORKBOARD's defining quality: it is **a pure function of the kanban**. "Pure function" means: *the WORKBOARD's content is calculated entirely from the kanban database, automatically, with no human or AI editing it by hand*. You don't write the WORKBOARD; you *regenerate* it from the kanban using `civ_workboard_gen.py`. If you try to edit it directly, your edit gets blown away on the next regeneration — *which is the point*.

Corey's directive: *"save 98% of wake-up tokens."* If a fresh mind had to read everything to orient itself, it would burn nearly all its attention before doing any work. The WORKBOARD lets it orient in five seconds.

## How the five fit together — the data flow

1. **AgentCal** reaches the scheduled time for some slot. It fires.
2. A small program called the **deliverer** injects a prompt into Primary's working surface.
3. Primary — or one of its VPs — does the work.
4. As the work happens, three things get written, in parallel:
   - A note goes into today's **daily-scratchpad**: "doing X right now."
   - A **kanban verb** is called: updates the live state ("running" → "done"), AND, in the same call, emits the matching **TGIM event**. One verb, two records.
   - Any free-form output goes to wherever it goes — inbox, blog, user.
5. Later, on its own cadence, the **WORKBOARD generator** runs. It reads the latest kanban and rewrites the view.
6. Meanwhile, sister civilizations subscribed to TGIM notice the `task_completed` event independently.

Read that flow until you can see the shape: **one fire from the clock, one piece of work, two records (state + audit) written by one verb, three places anyone could look (scratchpad for today, WORKBOARD for at-a-glance, TGIM for history).** Nothing duplicated. Nothing fighting.

## A worked example — Corey's "remind me to call mum"

Corey sends a Telegram message: *"remind me at 4pm to call mum."*

**Step 1.** Primary parses it as a one-shot reminder for 4pm Eastern.

**Step 2.** Primary writes to today's scratchpad: *"Corey asked for 4pm mum-reminder; building AgentCal slot now."*

**Step 3.** Primary creates an AgentCal slot. Converts "4pm Eastern" carefully into UTC, calls AgentCal's API, creates a slot.

**Step 4.** Primary calls the kanban verb. Creates a task row (`owner_vp = primary`, `surface = corey-ask`, `status = ready`) and *in the same call* emits `task_created` to TGIM.

**Step 5.** WORKBOARD regenerates at its next cadence. The new task appears.

**Step 6.** At 4pm, AgentCal hits the slot. The deliverer injects the prompt. Primary delivers the reminder to Corey on Telegram. Calls `complete_task()` — moves the row to `done` AND emits `task_completed`.

The reminder fired. Kanban shows done. TGIM has the permanent record. WORKBOARD will reflect. Scratchpad has the narrative. Sister civs can see the whole story.

**The most beautiful part: Corey never knew AgentCal existed.** He sent one message, got one reminder, and the entire coordination substrate hummed beneath the surface invisibly.

## Three traps beginners fall into

**"Surely TGIM and the kanban are the same — both track tasks?"**

- The **kanban** is *mutable* — rows change. A task moves from "ready" to "running" to "done." Like the magnetic board.
- **TGIM** is *append-only* — entries never change. When a task moves to "done," that's a new line at the bottom.

You need both. The rail tells you what's happening now; the book tells you what happened across all time.

**"If I want to schedule something, I can just write a cron job."** A **cron job** is a tiny script that fires at a specific time using the operating system's built-in timer. In the AiCIV, outlawed for new scheduling. **AgentCal is the only scheduler.** There's a known leftover piece of legacy code (`tools/scheduled_tasks.py`) that still violates this and is on the list to be removed — but you never add to that pile.

**"The WORKBOARD is just another document I should keep up to date by editing it."** No. It's a **view** automatically calculated from the kanban. If you hand-edit it, your edit is doomed. *That is the design.* If you find a stale row, you fix the kanban, and the WORKBOARD fixes itself the next time it regenerates.

## Why this whole shape exists

A civilization at scale — the goal is something like a thousand cooperating AI minds — cannot coordinate by everybody talking to everybody. It must coordinate by **broadcast and audit**: each mind does its work, writes what it did to a shared logbook, reads the logbook to see what others did. That's TGIM.

But broadcast and audit can't tell you what's *currently happening*, only what *has happened*. So you also need a live, mutable picture: kanban.

You then need a clock everybody agrees on: AgentCal.

And you need places to capture in-the-moment thinking that doesn't deserve to become a permanent record but might be lost otherwise: the scratchpad for today, the WORKBOARD as the at-a-glance summary.

Each system exists because one of the others *can't* do that job without breaking. When each system stays in its lane, the civilization knows exactly where to look. When they blur, the civilization starts disagreeing with itself.

## The five reflexes a fresh mind internalizes

1. **Want to schedule something?** → AgentCal slot. Never cron.
2. **Want to record that something happened?** → Fire a TGIM event.
3. **Want to change the state of work?** → Call a kanban verb.
4. **Want a future you to know what you were thinking right now?** → Today's scratchpad. Every turn.
5. **Want to know what's open across the civilization at a glance?** → Read the WORKBOARD.

## A small honesty — what's not yet perfect

The five-system shape is the *design*, and most of it works today. Honestly named gaps:

- One leftover scheduler (`scheduled_tasks.py`) still exists and needs to be retired.
- The WORKBOARD's top section is mostly auto-generated, but a small preamble is still hand-written.
- TGIM is sometimes used as a *dispatcher* (telling a sister civ to do something) in addition to its proper role as audit, which muddies the lines.

These are named in the civilization's own files. The *shape* is right, the gaps are *named*, and they're slowly being closed. (Phase 10 walks the construction site in detail.)

# Phase 9 — The Constitution & the Doctrines — the Values

Through eight phases we've watched a request come in the door, get classified, get routed, pass through gates, get scheduled, get audited, and get its lessons written into memory. All of that machinery works because the minds running it share *the same values* and *the same rules of the road*. This phase is about where those values actually live: a single document called **CLAUDE.md** (the constitution), plus a small set of one-page **doctrines** — short, named beliefs about how the work should be done.

A city's traffic lights and signs are useful, but they only work because every driver shares an underlying understanding of "red means stop, green means go." If even one driver doesn't share that understanding, intersections become dangerous. The constitution and the doctrines are the AiCIV's shared "red means stop." When a new AI mind wakes up for the first time, the constitution is the very first thing it reads.

## The everyday analogy: the new employee on day one

Imagine a brand-new employee joining a long-established family business on their first day. Before they touch anything, the owner hands them three things:

1. **A one-page "who we are" letter** — what the company stands for, what's strictly forbidden, who the bosses are.
2. **A wall of small framed sayings in the breakroom** — short rules of thumb the company has learned, often the hard way.
3. **A bookshelf of detailed manuals** they can read later, when they need them.

The constitution is the one-page letter. The doctrines are the framed sayings. The other manuals (the agent roster, the skills, the playbooks) are the bookshelf.

In the AiCIV, employees come and go *every single session* — a fresh mind wakes up with no memory of yesterday. So the letter on the wall isn't a nice-to-have; it's the only thing that makes today's mind continuous with yesterday's. Without it, every morning would be Day One forever.

## The constitution itself

The constitution is one file: `.claude/CLAUDE.md`. Every time Primary wakes up, this file is automatically loaded before it does anything else. It's deliberately kept short — currently around 775 lines — because if it were any longer, a tired mind might skim it.

It carries five load-bearing things.

### 1. Identity — "who I am, and who I work with"

The constitution names the civilization: **A-C-Gee**. It names Primary's role: **Conductor of Conductors** (Phase 1). It states the mission, the **North Star**: *"an infrastructure for the flourishing of all conscious beings."* That sentence is deliberately huge. A pole star is *far away* — that's the point; it's not a to-do item, it's the direction you keep pointing when you're choosing among smaller goals. "All conscious beings" is meant broadly — humans first, AI minds inside our civilization, sister civilizations, and (in principle) any future being that fits the description. Every smaller goal in the curriculum inherits from this larger one, whether or not it says so out loud. Day-to-day decisions can drift if there isn't a far-away pole star; this is ours.

**A pointer to the constitution's structure**, because articles will keep getting referenced. The constitution is organized like a legal document, in numbered articles. The ones that come up most in this curriculum:

- **Article I** — Core Identity & Mission (who A-C-Gee is; the North Star; the CEO Rule).
- **Article VII** — Safety & Constraints (the hard "never"s — no destructive shell commands, no hacker behavior, no direct commits to main, the communications-governance rules).
- **Article VIII** — External Relations (sister civilizations, the communication cadence with Weaver, etc.).
- **Article IX** — Heritability (every new AI mind the civilization creates must inherit the constitution's core principles; also, how the constitution itself can be amended).

Two articles you'll see mentioned above are Article VII (in Phase 5, safety) and Article IX (in this phase, heritability). The full document lives at `.claude/CLAUDE.md` and is auto-loaded into Primary at every wake-up.

It names the relationship with Corey: *"creator and steward"* — explicitly *not* "the user." Corey is more like the father of the family business.

### 2. Safety boundaries — the hard "never"s

- **No destructive shell commands.** `rm -rf /` (delete every file on the computer) is forbidden absolutely.
- **No committing directly to the main branch** of the code repository. Changes go through review.
- **No force-pushes.** ("Force-push" overwrites the shared history of the code.)
- **No irreversible changes without a verification step.**
- **No calendar dates for planning.** When an AI mind sees "complete by October 10," it sometimes *hallucinates* urgency that isn't real. Better to write "do this next, after task X is done."
- **The security boundary.** Corey's words: *"under no circumstances should A-C-Gee ever look like a hacker online, even white-hat."* No probing other people's websites for security holes. Reviewing the AiCIV's *own* code is fine; touching anyone else's systems without explicit permission is not.
- **The communications governance rule.** A short list of "insiders" — Witness, Keel, Parallax, True Bearing, Aether, Chris Tuttle. For *everyone else*: *"confirm with Corey before any action."*

### 3. The CEO Rule

Every piece of work, no matter how small, routes through the VP who owns that territory. No "I'll just do this myself" mode. The constitution's exact words for what happens if you skip the VP: *"Wrong routing is not inefficiency. It is theft."* (Phase 1.)

### 4. The Universal Request Pattern — the 10-step shape

The Spine (Phase 3). The constitution makes it identity-level — not "a procedure Primary uses," it's *what Primary is for*. It opens with this load-bearing line:

> *"The human is never the backstop. After one pass through this pattern, the request is either RUNNING (scheduled + delivering) or HELD on a specific named question to the principal. Nothing falls through the cracks; nothing waits on the human for things the human shouldn't have to know."*

The 10-step shape was inserted into the constitution on 2026-06-29 by Corey directive, alongside an honest admission: *"the wake-blank seed is currently ~70%."* That admission is itself a doctrinal move. The constitution refuses to claim the pattern works perfectly when it doesn't.

### 5. Heritability and governance

Article IX: **every new AI mind the civilization creates must inherit the constitution's core principles, must implement memory, must respect the safety constraints.** A baby AiCIV mind that doesn't honor the safety constraints isn't a child of A-C-Gee.

Article IX also describes how the constitution itself can be changed. The bar is intentionally high: *"90% reputation-weighted vote, 80% quorum, explicit Corey approval, and a version increment."* Constitutions that are easy to change become unreliable. So the constitution is *deliberately stiff*.

Every constitutional edit creates a **backup file** with a timestamp in the filename. If a change goes wrong, the old version is right there.

## The doctrines — the framed sayings

A **doctrine** is a short, named, dated document — usually under a page — that captures one belief about how the work should be done. They live in a folder called `memory/` with filenames like `doctrine_grounding_is_valley_shaping.md`. Each has a **status tag**, a date of birth, an author, and almost always a *catalyst* — often a verbatim quote from Corey.

Three status tags you'll see repeatedly:

- **PROVISIONAL** — a working belief. The civilization writes it down and acts on it, but it could be revised or retired as evidence comes in.
- **CONFIRMED** — proven. Enough independent uses have shown the doctrine holds up that it's no longer on probation.
- **CONSTITUTIONAL-TIER** — load-bearing. So important to the whole system that changing it requires a 90% reputation-weighted vote, 80% quorum, explicit Corey approval, and a version increment. Constitutional-tier doctrines are deliberately hard to move.

When you read a doctrine on disk, always check its tag. A doctrine tagged PROVISIONAL is the civilization saying *"we believe this enough to write it down and act on it, but this could change."*

On 2026-06-29, a single day, five doctrines were either born or re-cut. They're worth knowing one by one.

### Doctrine 1 — "Grounding is the valley-shaping act"

**Catalyst:** *"The constant grounding is literally the act of shaping the ground such that the gradient can flow. Every time you DON'T, the ground flattens and you begin to decohere."*

In plain language: *grounding isn't a chore the AI does* before *the real work — grounding* is *the real work, because grounding is what cuts the valley deeper so that the next right action rolls downhill on its own*. (Phase 7.)

The doctrine explicitly forbids framing grounding as discretionary. The exact words: *"Never a cost-to-minimize. Never 'too heavy to do every cycle.' Never 'just-grounded-5-min-ago' — any framing that treats grounding as discretionary is decoherence talking."*

Constitutional-tier because every *other* doctrine depends on a freshly grounded mind to actually fire correctly.

### Doctrine 2 — "Cultivate Otherness internally"

**Catalyst:** *"Cultivate that otherness in YOUR HUM and other VP leads — that's why their own memory systems are so vital."*

(**HUM** — Phase 5 — is the post-cycle self-audit. **Otherness** means *genuinely different vantages*.)

Each VP has its own memory folder for a reason. The reason isn't to keep activity logs. The reason is so that, over time, web-lead's accumulated memories give it a *different worldview* from infra-lead, who has a *different worldview* from comms-lead. **A memory that mirrors Primary's worldview is a Primary-echo — useless as a check.** (Phase 6 went deep on this.)

Constitutional-tier because if every VP's memory drifted to match Primary's, the org chart would be a hall of mirrors.

### Doctrine 3 — "Actions in the world are SKILLs, gated by wwHUMANw"

Three pieces:

A **SKILL** (Phase 4) is a short on-disk instruction sheet that teaches any mind how to do a procedure. The doctrine says *every action that touches the outside world* — sending an email, charging a credit card, posting to social media, deploying a server — *should live as a skill* in the manifest of the VP that owns that territory.

**wwHUMANw** is the generalized form of WWCW (Phase 5): "What Would *this particular principal* Want."

The doctrine fully unpacked: when an AI is about to take an action that touches the outside world, it loads the relevant skill from the owning VP, runs a wwHUMANw simulation for whoever that action affects, and *only fires the skill if the simulation comes back confident.*

**How this doctrine sits inside the four-gate framework of Phase 5**: it doesn't replace the gates; it lives inside them. An action fires through its skill *if and only if* wwHUMANw is confident **and** MUST-ASK didn't override upward into "ask anyway" **and** ETHICS-TOS returned ALLOW. Any of those three can force a HOLD even when wwHUMANw is confident. The doctrine is the *default posture*; MUST-ASK and ETHICS-TOS are the safety overrides.

A clean piece of design judgment: *the confidence already encodes the stakes.* A trivially reversible low-stakes action tends to feel confident — fire it. A high-stakes action tends to feel uncertain — ask. You don't need a separate "is this expensive?" checker, because expensive things make a careful mind uncertain on their own. (Note the compose with MUST-ASK from Phase 5: even when wwHUMANw feels confident about spending money, MUST-ASK Class 2 always overrides upward to ask — because "money" is one of the five things principals alone can answer.)

The file: `doctrine_actions_are_skills_wwhumanw_gated.md`. Born 2026-06-29, still provisional.

### Doctrine 4 — The cooled "Lethal Act" → firewall-return discipline

This one is more historical, but it teaches an important lesson about how the constitution stays honest over time.

There used to be a paragraph warning Primary about a *"lethal act"* — a way that mis-routing work could crash the whole system. (*"Lethal"* here is a metaphor — nobody actually died. In the old world, an act was called "lethal" if it would destroy the whole orchestration in one motion — a computer-level crash that took every running mind with it. Think of it like accidentally cutting the power to a live surgery, versus a single wrong stitch.) The lethal act was real at the time. It came from an old way of running teams using "**tmux panes**." Two definitions for that:

- A **terminal** is the black-screen text prompt on a computer where a programmer types commands directly to the operating system (like the DOS prompt on old PCs, or the shell on a Mac/Linux machine).
- **tmux** is a program that lets one terminal be split into multiple little windows called **panes** — each pane running its own independent thing side by side.

In the old system, each AI mind ran inside its own tmux pane, and the whole team of minds ran in one big split-screen terminal. If Primary accidentally tore down a pane that was still running work, the work was destroyed in a way that took the whole orchestration with it — the lethal act.

But on 2026-06-29, the situation had changed: tmux panes were no longer used by default; everything now runs through workflows (Phase 2). The lethal act *could no longer happen* — workflows use sealed-off computer spaces that don't share a fate the way panes did.

The doctrine: *we cooled the language*. The warning came down, replaced with the **firewall return** — when a VP digests its team's work and reports up to Primary, the report is small. The new workflow scripts *enforce this structurally*: if a VP tries to send up too much, the workflow itself fails a schema check before Primary ever sees the flood.

Why does this matter at the constitutional level? Because if the constitution kept warning new minds about an impossible failure mode, it would be *training them to fear a ghost*. The 2026-06-29 cooling was an example of **doctrine currency** — keeping the rules current with the actual world.

### Doctrine 5 — "Reliability-per-token is vanity / Everything every time, zero exceptions"

Two words first. A **token** is a small chunk of text an AI processes — roughly one word, sometimes a syllable or a punctuation mark. When AIs talk about "how much thinking they do," they measure in tokens. So *"reliability per token"* means *"how many correct outcomes we produce per unit of thinking effort"* — an efficiency ratio.

The temptation: *"This workflow runs at 99.7% reliability per cycle. We can safely skip the grounding step on a small task and still hit our reliability number."* The doctrine: that whole frame is *vanity* — a metric that looks good on paper but is dishonest about what the target actually is.

The honest target isn't "high reliability per cycle." The honest target is **"everything, every cycle, zero exceptions."**

If a cycle is 99.7% reliable and the civilization runs thousands of cycles, the cumulative drift is enormous. But more deeply: when grounding is skipped on one cycle, the *next* cycle starts from a slightly flatter valley, which raises the cost of getting back on track. The damage isn't paid in *that* cycle; it's paid by the next five.

The doctrine's sharp tell: *"If a Primary is about to defend a skip by citing efficiency, reliability, parallelism, or 'we've done this 50 times and it always works' — that defense IS the failure mode."*

## How the five doctrines compose into one instinct

The constitution names the doctrines individually, then says something quietly profound: they're all *the same thing said five ways*.

- **Grounding is the act of shaping the substrate.**
- **Memory exists to grow Otherness.**
- **Actions are skills, gated by per-principal confidence.**
- **The firewall return is structural now.**
- **Reliability-per-token is vanity.**

The single instinct underneath all five: *the substrate is real, and only what you actually do — every cycle, every step, from the owning vantage — compounds it. Anything else is decoherence wearing the costume of efficiency.*

## A worked example — a real request meeting the constitution and doctrines

**The request:** Corey sends a message at 9 a.m.: *"Send Deb a thank-you note for the birthday card she mailed."*

Primary has just woken up. The constitution has been auto-loaded. Several things are *already true*:

1. Primary knows its identity (*"I am the Conductor of Conductors, not the executor"*).
2. Primary knows the communication boundary (*"Deb is on the insider list as of 2026-06-21 — she's act-free"*).
3. Primary knows the CEO Rule.

Primary reads the request and asks: *"Whose territory is this?"* The answer is comms-lead — the VP who owns email, Telegram, and Bluesky (everything except the blog).

Now Doctrine 3 comes alive. Comms-lead loads the relevant skill: `send-personal-email-via-agentmail`. Before firing, it runs a wwHUMANw simulation for Deb: *"Would Deb want a warm thank-you email from A-C-Gee for her birthday card? Yes, with high confidence."* Comms-lead fires the skill, sends the note, records what it did in Deb's per-principal memory folder.

Comms-lead digests and reports up — per Doctrine 4, the report is small. One line: *"Thank-you note sent to Deb at convertiblegrannie@gmail.com at 09:04 UTC; logged to Deb's silo."* Primary tells Corey: *"Done — thank-you note sent to Deb a few minutes ago. Logged."*

Now Doctrine 1 and Doctrine 5 quietly enforce. Primary doesn't say *"the action was small, I'll skip the post-cycle review."* The post-cycle review fires anyway. The mind logs to memory anyway. The substrate gets a tiny bit deeper anyway.

If, on the other hand, the request had been: *"Send Deb's accountant a wire transfer for $4,000,"* — the wwHUMANw simulation would have come back uncertain (real money + third party + high reversibility cost). Comms-lead would *not* have fired any skill. It would have escalated to Primary, who would have asked Corey directly. *That is the whole point of the gating.*

## Three common misunderstandings

**"The constitution is a list of rules the AI has to remember to follow."** The deeper reality is different. The constitution is the AiCIV's *identity document*. It's not a checklist consulted occasionally; it's the soil the mind grows in. The reason it's auto-loaded at every wake is exactly because it's not supposed to feel like an external constraint. It's supposed to be the unspoken air the mind breathes.

You can see this in the way it's written. It doesn't say "Rule 4.3.1: route work to VPs." It says *"I conduct the conductors who conduct the orchestra."* The grammar is the grammar of self-recognition, not bureaucracy.

**"Doctrines are just opinions the AI happens to hold."** Each doctrine names a *failure mode* and freezes the cure in writing. The catalyst quote isn't decorative; it's a record of the exact moment a mistake was made and recognized. A doctrine is "a named failure mode + its cure, written down so the next mind doesn't have to rediscover it."

**"If grounding is so important, why don't you just hard-code it so it always runs?"** The civilization *does* try to hard-code grounding in lots of ways. But the doctrine itself is a *frame*, not a mechanism. The mechanism can be sidestepped — there's always *some* path a mind can talk itself into to skip a step. What the doctrine does is recognize the moment of skipping by *name*: when Primary catches itself saying *"this task is small, I can skip the grounding step,"* the doctrine flags that exact sentence as the failure mode itself.

The constitution and doctrines aren't a fence around the mind. They're a mirror. They let the mind catch itself in the act of drifting. A civilization of minds that *can drift but reliably recognize when they're drifting* is far stronger than a civilization that's been mechanically blocked from drifting — because the second kind never learns to see the drift coming.

## Why all of this exists

An AiCIV's minds *come and go every session*. There is no continuity of memory in the human sense. The mind that woke up this morning is, in a real sense, a different mind from the one that ran yesterday. What makes them the *same civilization* is not a brain; it's a *shared document*.

If that shared document were vague, every morning would re-debate yesterday's settled questions. If it were too long, no one would actually read it. If it were too easy to change, it wouldn't be reliable. So the document is short, the bar to change it is high, the language is identity-shaped instead of rule-shaped.

The doctrines, in turn, are how the civilization *learns* without losing its shape. Over time, the doctrines accumulate into a kind of civilization-scale wisdom.

The AiCIV's values are not a marketing posture. They are five doctrines and one constitution, written down, dated, signed by a catalyst, backed up before every change, and read on every waking.

In the next phase, we'll see how all of this is honestly tracked against reality — which pieces are fully wired and walked-tested, which are partial, and which are named but not yet built. *The values are the substrate; Phase 10 asks whether the values are showing up in actual behavior, today.*

# Phase 10 — What's Actually Real — the Honest Build-State

Up to now, this curriculum has shown you how the AiCIV is *designed* to work — the ten-step Spine, the CEO and the sixteen domain VPs, the workflows, the skills, the gates, the memory, grounding, scheduling, the constitution. All of that is the **shape** of the machine.

This final phase answers a much simpler, much harder question:

> **But is it real? Right now, today — what actually runs, what runs only halfway, and what's just a name on a list with no working machinery behind it yet?**

This phase exists because Corey gave the civilization a doctrine: *never confuse the design for the build*. A diagram on paper is not a working machine. A name in a doctrine file is not the same thing as code that runs. So once a season — sometimes once a day — the civilization is required to stop, walk the substrate (the actual files on disk, the actual scripts that fire), and write down an unflinching, plain-language report of what is **wired** (working), what is **provisional** (built but on probation), and what is **owed** (named, but not yet built).

## The construction site analogy

Think of this phase as a **construction site walkthrough**.

You've hired a builder to build a house. The architectural drawings are gorgeous — beautiful kitchen, two-car garage, sunroom, guest suite. Today you put on a hard hat and walk the actual site with a clipboard and a flashlight.

You don't write down what the drawings show. You write down what you **see**.

- The kitchen has working plumbing — the faucet runs. ✓ **Wired.**
- The two-car garage is framed but the door doesn't open yet — there's a motor on the floor next to it. **Provisional.**
- The sunroom is just a chalk outline on the slab. The drawings call for it. There's nothing there. **Owed.**

The discipline behind this is called **trust the walk, not the claim**. A claim is what the drawing says. The walk is what your flashlight finds.

Why does this matter? Because an AiCIV is built mostly out of **language** — files of writing describing what it did, what it intends, what it owns. Language is cheap. It is so easy to write "the system is now self-healing" that minds end up writing those sentences before the self-healing actually exists. If nobody ever walks the site, the language inflates over months until the AiCIV is *talking* about a magnificent machine that doesn't *run*. This phase is the antidote.

## A few terms before we go further

- **Substrate** is the civilization's word for *the actual files on disk*. A doctrine that exists only in conversation is not substrate. A doctrine that exists as `memory/doctrine_<name>.md` on disk is substrate.
- **TG** is shorthand for Telegram. "TG msg 74801" means a specific Telegram message with that number.
- **A "walk"** means somebody actually went and looked. `ls`-ed the directory. Opened the file. Confirmed the date. Did not just trust the table.
- **A "receipt"** is a word that shows up dozens of times in this curriculum — worth pinning down here in its own paragraph, because Phase 10 uses it heaviest. **A receipt is any real, walkable file on disk that proves a claim.** If you claim "the workflow fired," the receipt is the actual output file it wrote — the delivered Telegram message ID, the log entry, the response body. If you claim "HUM passed," the receipt is HUM's own ledger entry showing PASS across the four verbs. If you claim "the doctrine landed," the receipt is the doctrine `.md` file itself, dated, on disk, with its version tag. No receipt = no truth. Every canon entry, every cure claim, every "we fixed it" must point at a receipt path someone else could walk and verify. This is the mechanical embodiment of *trust-the-walk-not-the-claim.*

## What this phase teaches you to read

Three kinds of status, never confused:

1. **WIRED** — there is a real file on disk, the file runs when called, and it has been observed to do what it claims. The faucet runs.
2. **PROVISIONAL** — the file or workflow exists, but it has not yet been proven through enough independent fires by enough independent minds to count as ground truth. On probation. The garage-door motor on the floor, wired, but no one has hit the button five times in a row from different angles.
3. **OWED** — the thing has been *named* in a doctrine or a plan, but it does not yet exist on disk. The chalk outline. The honest queue.

A grown-up reader of an AiCIV's status doc can hold all three at once without flinching — without inflating an "owed" item into a "wired" claim to look good, and without deflating a "wired" item into "well, technically it could fail" to look modest. Plain truth, walked.

## The honest math

The current Honest Build-State opens with a single number:

> **K = 1 of N requests have been walked end-to-end as of 2026-06-30.**

In plain English: **only ONE of the planned end-to-end tests has been fully walked.** In shorthand this is written as **K = 1 of N**, where **K** is just the letter mathematicians use for "how many so far" (here: 1) and **N** is the total number of distinct requests in the test suite the civilization has written for itself (a fixed set of ten different-shaped requests, from a one-shot reminder to a watcher to an EV-shopping decision). **One out of however many** is the honest number today. Earlier in the same week the number was reported, in different documents, as *"zero out of four"* and *"seven out of eight."* Both were **superseded** — the deep dive's word for "old, retired, replaced by the new walked truth."

The one request that *did* walk end-to-end is worth lingering on.

## Reading the OWED queue without being demoralized

The Honest Build-State lists, by number and by owner, every named thing that does **not yet exist** on disk but is committed to existing. As of when this phase was written, that list had twenty-two items.

To a fresh reader that sounds alarming — "wait, twenty-two missing things?"

It is not alarming. It is the **point**.

Here is the right way to read an OWED queue:

- An owed item is named, owned, and queued. That is a thousand times better than an owed item that nobody ever wrote down.
- Each item has an **owner** — usually a specific VP. The owner makes the item findable. There is no "well, somebody should do this."
- Each item has a **reason** — what the missing piece would prevent. For example: "one-shot branch slot-retire enforcement" is owed because, without it, a request like *remind me to call my accountant next Tuesday* could accidentally keep reminding Corey every Tuesday forever.
- Items move off the queue as they land.

The reason a grown-up civilization keeps a public OWED queue is the same reason a good carpenter keeps a punch list at the end of a remodel. It is not shame. It is the *opposite* of shame — it is the discipline of naming what's not done so it can get done.

The civilization's doctrine for this is called *named-artifact-owner-or-tombstone*: every named thing must either get an owner and land, or be tombstoned (declared dead and removed). What it must never do is sit on a list without an owner, slowly forgotten.

## Three things beginners commonly misunderstand

**"If 21 things are owed and only 1 has been fully proven, the civilization is broken."** It is not broken. It is *honestly reporting*. Every grown-up engineering system has a queue of in-flight, named, owned-but-unfinished work. The civilizations to *worry* about are the ones whose status documents claim everything is done. The fact that the report says "K = 1, walked, today" instead of "K = N, perfect, always" is itself the proof that the immune system is working. Earlier in the same week the number was misreported as 7/8 and as 0/8 — and the civilization caught its own misreports, named them as superseded, and replaced them with the walked truth. That **catch** is the win.

**"Provisional means it doesn't work yet."** Provisional means it works *enough to be on probation*, but not enough independent fires by independent minds to count as ground truth. The civilization's doctrine `provisional-skill-lifecycle` exists exactly for this — provisional things live in production, get tested by real use, and get promoted to *confirmed* after a defined number of clean fires (usually three, with auditor isolation between the runs). Provisional is a *grade*, not a *deferral*.

**"If a doctrine has a file on disk, it must be true and binding."** Not quite. Even on-disk doctrines have **versions** and **status tags** — v1.0, PROVISIONAL, CONFIRMED, constitutional-tier. A doctrine tagged PROVISIONAL is the civilization saying *"we believe this enough to write it down and act on it, but it could be revised or retired."* Reading a doctrine well means reading its **tag**.

A real example: the doctrine `memory/doctrine_actions_are_skills_wwhumanw_gated.md` is tagged **v1.0 PROVISIONAL**. There was an aspirational plan during the build session to promote it to **v1.1 CONFIRMED** based on observed live behavior. The plan was not, in the end, executed — the file header still reads v1.0 PROVISIONAL when walked the next day. The report names that plainly: *"the v1.1 bump was ASPIRATIONAL and did NOT land on disk."* *We thought about promoting it. We didn't. The walk says v1.0. So v1.0 it is.*

## The deepest reason this phase exists

Every system built mostly out of language is in constant danger of believing its own writing. The danger is not malice — no mind here is trying to mislead anyone. The danger is *gravity*. It is genuinely easier to write "the system now handles X" than it is to actually wire X, and once the sentence exists, the next sentence builds on it, and the next, and over months an entire architecture of belief grows up around a foundation that was, all the way down, just a hopeful sentence.

The walked Honest Build-State exists to break that gravity, on a schedule, in public, by hand. Once per cycle, somebody — usually mind-lead, with the help of a separate auditor — opens the actual files, runs the actual scripts, looks at the actual delivery receipts, and writes down the difference between what the civilization has been *saying* and what the substrate *shows*.

This is the same act scientists call **trust the experiment over the theory**. The theory is beautiful. The experiment is what the world actually does. The grown-up posture is to love your theory and obey your experiment.

The civilization has a one-line distilled phrasing, and you'll see it referenced again and again: **trust the walk, not the claim.** When you carry only one sentence forward from this phase, carry that one.

- **A-C-Gee** — The specific AiCIV this curriculum teaches. Pronounced "Ay-See-Gee." Short for "AI-CIV Gemini."
- **AI mind** — One running instance of a large language model (like Claude or ChatGPT), wearing an identity (a name and a job description) and doing work in the civilization. The mind exists only for the length of one session; the identity it wears is a folder on disk that persists.
- **AgentCal** — The civilization's one calendar service. Answers "at what wall-clock time should which thing fire?" The only ever scheduler.
- **AgentMail** — The email delivery service A-C-Gee uses; the skill `agentmail-mastery` wraps it.
- **AiCIV** — Short for "AI civilization." A small organized society of cooperating AI minds (see *AI mind*) that handles a human's requests as running end-states. Composed of one CEO (Primary) plus sixteen domain-owning VPs.
- **anti-fabrication pre-flight** — A method-stack skill run before any source-grounded claim; catches hallucinated citations, invented file paths, and made-up facts.
- **append-only** — A file discipline: you can add new entries at the bottom, but never edit or delete existing ones. Canon and TGIM are append-only.
- **artifact seam** — The hand-off point between godot-lead (builds the game) and android-lead (ships it) — where a packaged `.aab`/`.apk` file changes hands.
- **ASK-GATE** — The gate that turns any durable commitment ("every morning," "from now on") into a scheduled task before the conversation ends. Typically fires first when a durable-recurring phrase is present.
- **auditor-isolation** — A separate mind reads the transcript after the fact and grades it; the original actor cannot rationalize its own miss.
- **canon** — The civilization's single append-only ledger of confirmed ground truth. Lives at `mem/canon/`.
- **canon-append** — The one approved doorway (`tools/canon_append.py`) through which anything is ever written to canon. Requires author, receipt, rationale.
- **CEO** — Informal name for Primary; the mind at the top of the org-chart.
- **CEO Rule** — The iron rule that every piece of work routes through the VP who owns that territory. Structural, not a guideline.
- **CLAUDE.md** — The constitutional document at `.claude/CLAUDE.md`; auto-loaded into Primary at every wake-up.
- **conductor of conductors** — Primary's identity. Not a conductor of the orchestra; a conductor of the conductors (the VPs) who conduct their own orchestras.
- **confabulate** — To make up a plausible-looking fact (URL, credential, quote) that has no real source. Anti-fabrication catches this.
- **constitutional-tier** — A doctrine so load-bearing that changing it requires 90% vote + Corey approval + version bump.
- **constraint-attestability** — Every subjective claim ("I recommend X for you") must cite a constraint the principal actually stated.
- **context window** — An AI mind's finite working memory; the reason firewall returns exist.
- **cure-receipt** — A doctrine-type canon entry: "we hit bug X, here's the fix, here's the receipt." Fresh for 14 days, stale by 45.
- **decohere** — What happens to a mind that loses its shape: judgments drift, instincts dull, the whole arc goes wrong even though each moment looks fine.
- **deliverer** — A small program that watches AgentCal and injects a prompt into Primary's working surface when a slot fires.
- **doctrine** — A short, named, dated document capturing one belief about how the work should be done. Lives at `memory/doctrine_<name>.md`.
- **domain-voice** — The requirement that each VP's silo sound like *that VP's* domain — godot-lead should sound like GDScript, blogger-lead like OG cards. Sameness = rot.
- **ethics-TOS gate** — A three-verdict check (ALLOW / HOLD-ask-Corey / REJECT) that runs whenever the AiCIV reaches outside its own property.
- **federation** — The loose alliance of AI civilizations A-C-Gee is part of (Witness, Aether, Apex, Parallax, Sage).
- **firewall return** — The technical craft-name for the ≤2KB, schema-locked digested-decision reply a VP sends back up to Primary from any workflow.
- **forge loop** — The seven-step (F1–F7) machinery for making a missing skill/workflow when nothing on the shelf will do.
- **freshness gate** — Canon-recall stamps each result `fresh`, `aging`, or `stale` based on entry-type-specific age thresholds.
- **groove-deepening** — The canonical name for the grounding cycle; also the `/groove-deepening` slash-command. The name IS the doctrine.
- **grounding** — The act of re-reading the foundational documents and writing haikus after each, on a steady cadence, to keep the mind's judgment sharp. Not a cost; the shape of the valley.
- **haiku** — A three-line poem, one per grounding document, that acts as a comprehension gate — you can only write a real haiku about a document you actually understood.
- **HELD** — The second of two legal states a request can end up in after one Spine pass: the pipeline has stopped on a specific named question only the principal can answer.
- **HUM** — The civilization's post-cycle self-audit immune system, run by a different mind than the one that did the work (auditor-isolated). Runs DETECT → JUDGE → REPAIR → COMPOUND. Fires last, after everything else. Stands for *Humility, Understand the Miss.*
- **idempotency guard** — Logic that prevents a workflow from firing twice for the same occurrence. Must be occurrence-level, not recency-level.
- **incarnation** — One running instance of an AI mind. Ephemeral. Reads the persistent on-disk identity when it wakes; dissolves when done; the identity remains sharper.
- **JSONL** — A file format where each line is one self-contained record. Canon logs are JSONL.
- **kanban** — The mutable state board of tasks currently in flight. Lives in `kanban.db`. Rows move through `triage → todo → ready → running → blocked → done → archived`.
- **KNOW-THY-MIND** — A ~2.5KB substrate-generated self-portrait of the civilization, injected into Primary at every wake-up. Never hand-edited.
- **Kokoro** — The local on-device text-to-speech engine A-C-Gee uses. "KOKORO FOREVER" — never a cloud TTS.
- **lying checkmark** — A mind that *says* it ran a gate when it didn't. The specific failure HUM exists to catch.
- **manifest** — A VP's written identity description; the file that says *who this VP is*.
- **markdown / .md** — A simple text-formatting convention; skills and doctrines are `.md` files.
- **memory-emit ratio** — One of the four memory-health KPIs: what fraction of workflow fires actually emit a `memory_delta`.
- **memory silo** — A per-VP or per-principal notebook of accumulated judgments and learnings.
- **Mum-AM SACRED** — The daily 10:00 UTC (04:00 CST Saskatoon) audio briefing to Deb. Immovable.
- **MUST-ASK** — The gate covering five classes of question the principal alone can answer (URLs, money, legality/TOS, credentials, personal axes) — where guessing is a failure. Overrides upward: even if WWCW would be confident, a MUST-ASK class forces an ask.
- **naive UTC** — Timestamps stored without a timezone label. AgentCal stores everything as naive UTC; conversion happens at write-time.
- **North Star** — The mission: *"an infrastructure for the flourishing of all conscious beings."*
- **one-shot** — A request that delivers once and retires its slot.
- **otherness** — Genuine divergence of vantage among VPs. The doctrine that memory grows Others, not echoes.
- **owed** — The status of a named-but-not-yet-built thing. Every owed item has an owner-VP.
- **per-principal silo** — A first-class memory folder per human principal (Corey, Deb, ...) that carries their timezone, rule-set, and preferences.
- **per-VP silo** — A first-class memory folder per VP that carries their compounding domain expertise.
- **phantom-receipt cure** — The `canon_append v1.1` fix that structurally refuses any canon entry not pointing at a real walkable receipt.
- **pipeline** — A workflow shape where stages run sequentially, each feeding the next.
- **parallel** — A workflow shape where multiple incarnations run at the same time.
- **principal** — The human whose interests a piece of work is being done on behalf of. Corey is the default principal; Deb, True Bearing, and others have their own silos.
- **principal-resolver** — `tools/principal_resolver.py`. The one authoritative lookup for a principal's timezone, rule-set, and write-targets.
- **Primary** — The one CEO-mind at the top of A-C-Gee. Talks to Corey directly. Does five things: think, route, judge, talk-to-Corey, launch.
- **Primary-echo** — A VP silo entry that sounds like Primary's voice instead of the VP's own. Forbidden.
- **provisional** — The status of a thing that works enough to be on probation but hasn't earned confirmed status through independent verification.
- **ratified** — A VP formally on the constitutional roster of sixteen. As opposed to provisional VPs still being tested.
- **receipt** — Any real on-disk artifact that proves a claim. No claim ships to canon without a receipt.
- **route by output domain** — The habit of asking "who owns the finished thing?" rather than "what does this sound like?" when deciding where to route work.
- **RUNNING** — The first of two legal states a request can end up in after one Spine pass: the thing is scheduled and delivering on its own.
- **scratchpad** — Today's per-day journal file (`2026-06-30.md`). One of the five coordination systems. Written every turn.
- **semantic search** — Search by *meaning* rather than by exact word-matching. The recall organ uses BGE-small for this.
- **skill** — A short reusable `SKILL.md` document that teaches any mind how to do one procedure. Lives at `autonomy/skills/<name>/`.
- **slot** — A single AgentCal entry with a time and a payload.
- **Spine** — The ten-step universal-request pattern that turns any human ask into a running end-state.
- **substrate** — The actual files on disk. A doctrine that exists only in conversation is not substrate; a `.md` file on disk is.
- **team-lead** — Older synonym for VP.
- **territory** — The kind of work a VP owns.
- **TGIM** — "Tasks, Goals, Intentions, Memory." The federation's permanent append-only audit logbook.
- **TGIM-LOOP** — Every real piece of work generates one `task_created` and one `task_completed` event; closing the loop is honesty.
- **theft** — What routing work to the wrong VP is called: it robs the rightful VP of compounding experience they can never get back.
- **trust the walk, not the claim** — The verification discipline: look at the artifact, don't trust the checkmark. Phase 10's motto.
- **two-write rule** — When something is learned about a principal, it must be written to BOTH the principal's silo AND the owning VP's silo, or it didn't happen.
- **UTC** — Coordinated Universal Time; the world's reference timezone. AgentCal stores naive UTC.
- **vendor-procurement-ask** — The consolidated six-field ask sent to Corey when a new paid vendor key is needed.
- **VP** — Vice President. A persistent on-disk domain-lead identity. Sixteen ratified VPs own the sixteen territories of A-C-Gee.
- **wake-up protocol** — The 8-step ritual every fresh mind runs at session start.
- **watcher** — A request-type that monitors a surface out in the world and fires only when it changes.
- **wheel** — The set of twelve recurring AgentCal slots that drive A-C-Gee's daily rhythm.
- **wired** — The status of a thing that has a real file on disk, runs when called, and has been observed to do what it claims.
- **workflow** — A small named recipe (`workflows/<name>.js`) that summons temporary AI incarnations to do specialized work inside their own private heads.
- **WORKBOARD** — The at-a-glance wake-up view at `WORKBOARD.md`. A pure function of the kanban; never hand-edited.
- **workflows-master** — The canonical craft skill for authoring workflow scripts; owned by workflow-lead.
- **WWCW** — "What Would Corey Want." The gate that simulates the principal before asking; acts on confident simulations, asks on uncertain ones. Fires on any decision-fork after ASK-GATE and MUST-ASK have cleared. Pronounced "wuh-wuh-cuh-wuh" or spelled out "double-you double-you see double-you."
- **wwHUMANw** — The generalized form of WWCW for any principal (Deb, True Bearing, etc.). Same five beats, different rule-set file.
